## 1. Combinations and Tradeoffs.mp4

### Summary

An intermediary is fundamentally defined by the **unique combination of tools** it selects to manage healthcare costs and quality, such as network design, provider payment structures, and patient cost-sharing. Managers must navigate a complex **landscape of trade-offs**, deciding whether to be aggressive with utilization controls to lower premiums or more permissive to ensure provider and patient satisfaction. These strategic choices often function as **substitutes for one another**; for instance, a plan that implements strong financial incentives for doctors may successfully reduce its reliance on intrusive administrative reviews. Ultimately, the diversity of the healthcare market reflects how different organizations **mix and match these levers** to adapt to their specific goals and competitive environments.

**Keywords:** Healthcare Intermediary Tools, Provider Payment Methods, Utilization Management, Cost Sharing, Plan Design Tradeoffs

### Content

There are a lot of tools that intermediaries can use to influence care use and costs trying to achieve their various goals. One way of thinking about the identity of any particular intermediary, why and how it's different from others is to say an intermediary is defined by the specific tools it chooses to use and how it uses them. This won't be the only way of thinking about the identity of an intermediary, but it's one useful thing to try. So then, how does an intermediary handle the development and use of its network? Broad or narrow, open Close panel. How does it pay providers? Fee fee for service, capitation, DRGs, global budgets. Is it tight on payments to providers or more generous? Strong cost sharing for patients or not so strong or maybe none. Does it use a gatekeeper or not? Utilization review a lot, a little what kind? Does it expend a lot of effort on coverage decisions or go easier and accept more things? In some sense, this is what the people who run intermediaries are expected to be good at. Figuring out their context, their specific goals, the providers and people they have to work with, the other intermediaries they have to deal with or compete against, and so on, and then designing their approach to best meet the goals, mixing and matching existing tools, sometimes making new ones, and then adapting over time. As a result, you'll see many different intermediary structures reflecting many different combinations. It might be fair, at least up to a point, to say every intermediary can be unique in its particular approach. That said, though, there are some general patterns and trade-offs that come up that we can take notice of. First, plans can vary in their overall stance with respect to the tools. Some use many tools to manage utilization and steer toward lower costs and more efficiency and others use fewer. Of course, enrolles will tend to use more expensive care when the intermediaries are less active and so premiums or taxes or whatever will need to be higher. On the other hand, plans can get lower premiums or lower tax requirements if they're more aggressive with their use of the tools. But this might require trade-offs with the satis ction of their providers or their population. Second, it can be useful to think of the tools or combinations of tools as substitutes for each other, at least up to a point. If you use a lot of one, then you can maybe use less of another. I'll give an example. A plan may work a lot on developing provider networks that are the right size for their enrolly population and use provider payment incentives that encourage the physicians and others to be careful with the services they use. If this works, then the plan may have to rely less on things like prior authorization requ requirements and could maybe use low patient cost sharing since these might not be required to keep utilization and costs under control or or try to drive higher quality. On the other hand, a plan with a very broad network with limited incentives for physicians to pay attention to efficiency might need to expend more energy on utilization review and use more cost sharing to try and steer toward more efficient use and keep costs down. This was one example. If you look around, you'll find others cases where plans do a lot with one type of tool. and as a result back off on others. There are lots of different variations that you'll see.

## 2. Coverage Decisions.mp4

### Summary

Health insurance intermediaries manage healthcare spending by determining which medical services qualify for financial protection. While most plans aim to include **medically necessary** treatments, they often act as gatekeepers by excluding procedures that lack **proven efficacy** or are deemed experimental. Beyond clinical results, these organizations frequently employ **cost-effectiveness analyses** to weigh the clinical benefits of a new therapy against its financial impact. This evaluative process creates a tension between **controlling rising costs** and ensuring that patients maintain access to essential, high-value care. Ultimately, these coverage decisions serve as a powerful tool for **steering the healthcare system** toward efficiency, though the practice remains a subject of significant social and political debate.

**Keywords:** Coverage decisions, Medical necessity, Treatment efficacy, Cost effectiveness, Intermediary influence

### Content

Intermediaries can influence care use and costs by limiting the services the plan will cover. We sometimes say plans make coverage decisions. It's common for plans to seek to cover a pretty broad range of services. Generally, you think that they're trying to cover services that are medically necessary. That is services that medical professionals would commonly say are warranted or indicated for the patient. But there can be some leeway in that area and intermediaries can take a more or less permissive stance with regard to the services they'll cover. Some may not elect cover everything out there. Some coverage decisions could be based on efficacy. If there isn't good evidence that a treatment really works or maybe if something is still considered experimental, then a plan may not cover it. Another thing you'll see is coverage decisions based on costs or on a related criteria of cost effectiveness. Cost effectiveness refers to information comparing the benefits of a new treatment or any treatment to its costs. There are sophisticated tools for doing these analyses and they can create measures that help identify things that have strong benefits for the money, better value, or that don't produce a lot of benefit given the cost. Intermediaries can use this information in their decision-making. For example, when considering whether to cover a new therapy that's been introduced, they could get cost effectiveness information and then decide that they have some cut offs. If the value is too low, something doesn't look like it provides enough benefit in return for the cost, they could decide not to cover it. If you, the patient, then want the service, you can go get it with your own money, but the intermediary is not going to help pay the use of costs or cost effectiveness information in coverage decisions can come up and be applied in different areas. One area where questions about this have come up a lot lately is around new drugs where there can be some high price new treatments that raise interesting cost effectiveness questions. Different countries have pursued this to different extents. The NHS in the UK is an example of an intermediary that has really worked hard to generate cost effectiveness evidence and incorporate it in their decisions about what they'll cover. In other countries like the US, this is not common. Of Of course, making coverage decisions for costs, cost effectiveness, or any other reason can be controversial. They may help by steering toward efficient services or desirable services and helping manage costs ideally while maintaining access to important treatments. But getting the balance right and not overrestricting can of course be a challenge too.

## 3. Intermediaries Lessons for Innovators.mp4

### Summary

To identify meaningful opportunities for innovation, one must look toward the **data-rich environments** and **multifaceted operational challenges** of healthcare intermediaries. These organizations handle everything from **predictive analytics** for patient care to the intricate logistics of **claims processing and fraud detection**, creating a wide range of entry points for new solutions. Because their priorities shift based on their specific **market niche**, such as balancing cost-containment with the need for high-quality **provider relations**, a deep understanding of their unique goals is essential. Ultimately, the text serves as a roadmap for innovators to align their tools with the **diverse administrative and clinical needs** of these complex entities.

**Keywords:** Healthcare data insights, Intermediary activities, Payment design approaches, Claims processing, Innovation opportunities

### Content

We can draw some insights about the space for innovations from looking at intermediaries. Obviously, pay attention to their data. They generally have lots often from their work paying for care, but also sometimes from other efforts to track the characteristics of their members and their healthcare use and other things. Also note the variety of things that they're doing that they might be able to use help with. They're trying to do a bunch of things. They're trying to figure out which tools to use and how to use them. Well, which providers to contract with. Should they add new providers? to their networks, drop some that they already have. Can they provide feedback to the providers in their network to help them improve their performance? How to design payment approaches and how to figure out which will be best in any given situation? How to figure out which of their enrolles are about to use complex medical care that they could possibly help before it happens. Which requests for pre-authorization they should approve and which not, which hospital stays for their members are getting to be too long, how optimally to set cost sharing, and on and on. And they can have other goals and activities as well. In a marketing sense, private intermediaries have to figure out which employers or people might be their most likely customers and how to market to them. And we didn't talk a lot about it, but intermediaries also have to process loads of bills for medical care claims we often call them, and figure out which ones to pay and which ones not, which have errors and which not, which might be fraud and which not, and other related tasks. And they have to do this in a complex environment that's affected by their niches in the market. Some plans may be quite focused on minimizing costs overall, for example, and look for tools to help with that. Others may face a reality that reducing costs too much is not their main goal, and they may want to focus more on improving provider or patient relations. So, if you think you want to work with intermediaries, take a look at their activities and their niche, which can be very informative about what they might be interested in. And note that they're not all the same, but they all pretty much have a set of challenges they're facing and therefore opportunities for innovation.

## 4. Intermediaries and the Broad Challenges Facing Health Care Systems.mp4

### Summary

In the modern healthcare landscape, **intermediaries** must balance the competing demands of maintaining patient satisfaction, ensuring high-quality care, and controlling **rising medical costs**. These organizations navigate a central paradox where **insurance coverage** facilitates access to life-saving innovation but simultaneously incentivizes the **overuse of expensive treatments**. Beyond managing financial risks, intermediaries are tasked with addressing systemic **barriers to access**, such as geographical shortages and socioeconomic inequalities that hinder equitable delivery. Ultimately, they function as vital yet burdened navigators who must reconcile their **business imperatives** with broader social mandates to create a functional, sustainable health system.

**Keywords:** Health care costs, Intermediary success factors, Insurance coverage effects, Medical care quality, Healthcare access challenges

### Content

Intermediaries are trying to solve a complex problem. Figuring out how to work with patients and providers in ways that keep them happy and provide needed medical care while also keeping costs low enough. When they do the right combination of both, they set themselves up for success. Every intermediary has to do this in the context of their own specific situation, but they all have to do this in the context of important broader forces. One of the key ones is constant pressure from high and rising costs. This happens for a variety of reasons. One important one is the arrival of new treatments that can help patients but are often costly. Research suggests that this has put steady pressure on health care costs over time and created challenges that intermediaries find themselves in the middle of. One interesting dimension here is that insurance is probably part of the problem. Economists identify insurance as a factor that encourages people to want more expensive treatments and thus that facilitates their development and dissemination. When you're insured, you can get costly medical care without paying the full price. This is kind of the idea to make it possible for people to get the care they need, but then maybe we worry that sometimes people get a bit or even a lot more than they need because it's easily available. So insurance is funny that way. We need it to manage risk and keep the health care system functioning, but then it has the potential to turn around and contribute to our cost challenges. This is tricky. New innovations, any treatment really, might be really beneficial, even if it's expensive. We don't want to underuse things, but at the same time, not everything is, and there can be forces that would lead us to overuse care if we're not careful. Intermediaries often find themselves right in the middle of helping us all, society broadly, navigate this challenge. Intermediaries also find themselves in the middle of coping with the challenges of providing highquality care. Researchers and others have identified general challenges with providing highquality care, and the challenges grow with the increasing complexity of treatments. Intermediaries are not exactly the ones delivering health care. But at the same time, they often take actions that shape the care that's delivered and when and how and where, which can have important influences. We want them to help us cope with the challenges of quality and get this right. And we know that there are challenges with access that can come up. Some of our access problems, particularly in the US, have to do with people not getting good access to insurance coverage at all. That's obviously an issue that involves intermediaries, but it also involves other social and government policy choices. There are other access challenges that can arise, too, like making sure there are enough providers in the right places. Making sure people with different incomes or cultures or education levels can obtain and use care. Intermediaries often find themselves right in the middle of coping with these kinds of challenges, too. Questions about access to care are actually a nice illustration of another dimension of challenges faced by intermediaries. There are times when we want them to help solve broader social challenges and inequalities, some of which have at least some of their roots outside the health care system. Now, sometimes they can do that. Sometimes it's within their mandate, especially if they're public programs. But we also have to remember that in important ways, intermediaries also can have to function like businesses with budgets and imperatives to manage cost and risks. It's a complicated world that intermediaries inhabit and a complex set of goals that they have to juggle.

## 5. Intermediaries and their Goals.mp4

### Summary

Health care intermediaries, such as insurers and health plans, act as vital bridges that manage **risk pooling** and coordinate the complex relationships between patients, providers, and payers. Their primary mission involves balancing the **satisfaction of care recipients and clinicians** with the necessity of maintaining **financial sustainability**, whether through government taxes or private premiums. To remain viable, these organizations must navigate a delicate trade-off: they must facilitate access to quality services while simultaneously **controlling costs** and preventing the overuse of low-value care. Ultimately, the success of an intermediary depends on its ability to maintain a positive reputation and **political or market support** by harmonizing these competing demands of affordability and service quality.

**Keywords:** Health care intermediaries, Risk pooling, Provider payment arrangements, Patient coverage rules, Cost control management

### Content

Let's turn our attention to intermediaries. Intermediaries, which we sometimes call other things like plans, payers, insurers, and the like, are essential to the functioning of the health care system. They provide mechanisms for risk pooling, taking on risk from individuals in the population, which is an essential task in today's healthcare world. And they find themselves right in the middle of many of the most important interactions in healthcare, handling payments to providers and defining coverage rules that influence the ways that patients can use care. There are a wide variety of intermediaries out there in the health care systems around the world. Many countries operate public intermediaries, insurers or health plans run by or under the opaces of the government and others incorporate sometimes rely heavily on privatelyun intermediaries operating essentially as private businesses and both come in many flavors and combinations. We'll need to spend some time looking at this area from different angles. One good way to get started thinking about intermediaries is to get handle on their basic goals and the problems they're trying to solve. And a way to get at this is to have a look at the groups they have to work with. Intermediaries face three different groups which they have to work successfully with in order to fulfill their missions. The first is providers. Intermediaries have to work out arrangements with health care providers to provide structure for taking care of patients covered by the intermediary and getting paid when they do so. The second is patients needing healthcare. Intermediaries have to work out the terms under which they'll cover healthcare which often end up influencing things like what services patients can get and how easy it is for them to get the services. How they work with these two groups matters. In a basic sense, intermediaries have to get along at least well enough with providers and patients since they have to work together on an ongoing basis. And the ways they work with providers and patients can have a big influence on the way they work with the third group important to them, the people who pay for the coverage from them, their customers or their buyers, if you will. For Public intermediaries, this is often the government officials and ultimately the people of their country who pay the taxes and provide support for their operations. For private intermediaries, this is their customers, the people they're hoping who will buy their products. The ways intermediaries work with providers and patients will influence the satisfaction people have with their products. If the doctors and hospitals are mad because the intermediary rules or payment levels or whatever are not to their satisfaction, or if the patients are mad, then it's easy to end up with a bad reputation, which can make it hard to maintain your political support or hard to sell your products in a marketplace. If everyone's happy, things are easier. The ways intermediaries work with providers and patients also influences the amount of money they have to ask for. For a public intermediary, the amount of money they need to get from the taxpayers, let's say, is closely related to how much they expect to pay out to providers for the care of covered patients. For private intermediaries, the premiums they have to charge are also closely related to the amount they expect to pay for the care of their enrollies. The more expensive the care, the higher your premiums have to be. Now, generally, the more you have to get from taxpayers or the more you have to charge in premiums, the harder it's going to be to be a successful intermediary, maintaining support or selling your insurance products. So, what are intermediaries trying to do then? They're trying to figure out ways to work with patients and providers that both maintain those relationships and keep people happy with the care they can get or the care they can provide and the terms under which they can do that and they also have to keep costs under control. Pay the providers enough but not too much. Make rules so patients can get the care they want or need but that still discourage overuse of lowv value things. Successfully do this and get the right balance and you survive as an intermediary. You retain the support of your population or you can successfully sell your products. Blow it in some dimension and you're in trouble.
