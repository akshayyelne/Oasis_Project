## 1. AI and incentives in health care delivery and payment structures.mp4

### Summary

Modern healthcare systems operate under complex **financial incentives** designed to manage costs and efficiency, which has created a critical need for **predictive analytics** to measure risks. Artificial intelligence has become an essential tool in this environment because of its unique ability to process **large-scale datasets** and identify patterns that traditional methods might miss. However, because these models often rely on **proxy measures** rather than direct indicators, they risk introducing **systematic bias** and discrimination against vulnerable populations. Ultimately, designers must possess deep clinical knowledge to ensure that AI models are **risk-adjusted accurately**, preventing financial priorities from causing unintended medical or ethical harm to patients.

**Keywords:** Healthcare incentive structures, AI predictive analytics, Financial risk modeling, Data proxy bias, Ethical model design

### Content

Large complex health systems exist in an environment of explicit incentives intended to drive behavior of patients and health care providers. For example, increasing co-ayments for the health care services that they get, such as emergency care, has been shown to decrease utilization of these services dramatically. These incentives are often for the purpose of improving the efficiency of healthcare. If that's your goal, there are two ways of achieving it. First, you could improve the quality of health care or second you could reduce health care costs or the utilization of healthcare resources. What does this have to do with AI? The existence of incentive structures that are built into health care systems through payment models means that physicians, hospitals, and insurers need to measure things related to the quality of care, costs, and utilization. These incentive structures have also led to classifying and predicting features of patients and patient care. It turns out that AI is particularly good at classifying and predicting, especially using really large data sets and especially in terms of probabilities. So AI based predictive analytics are feverishly being applied to health data for classifying and predicting. These AI models like more traditional predictive modeling use statistical techniques to correlate features of patients such as vital signs or diagnosis with outcomes such as mortality or length of stay in the hospital. But there are now so-called big data on really large numbers of patients and huge amounts of electronic information. These data can be collected from individual patients from medical records, digital images and monitors. This means not only that AI would be useful for analyzing all these data but actually necessary for improving the accuracy of classification and prediction. So AI based models can be used to help health care systems, providers and insurers to classify and predict things that could be used to allow them to respond to the incentives I just talked about. That is the models are designed to predict or identify sources of risk where risk means the probability of harm. Here one way I'm defining risk is as the probability of facing a financial loss associated with the use of healthcare. An AI can be particularly good at identifying risk that is unexpected or less obvious, but as I'll talk about next, has limitations when applied to health data. And these limitations have ethical implications. Another type of risk is the probability of medical harm associated with the use of healthcare. This type of harm could come from medical error in diagnosis or treatment or from under treatment or over treatment based on uncertainty or because of incentive structures. However, note that financial risks are largely measured and predicted from the perspective of health care systems, providers, and insurers. That means they are considering mainly financial risk to themselves, not to patients. So, what are some of the examples of applications that use AI based predictive analytics? Here's one. An insurance company builds an AI model that uses insurance claims, electronic health records, and consumer data to predict which of its members are likely to incur the highest cost of care over the next year. An ethical challenge in building this model arises because we know that vulnerable populations such as the poor, people with disabilities, and people with racial and ethnic minorities tend to incur disproportionately high health care costs. But that could be because these populations are sicker when they do enter the health care system and thus need emergency or specialty care. This could in turn be because the populations have low access to preventive or primary care. In order to minimize the chances that predictive models discriminate against patients in these vulnerable groups, it's important for the model to risk adjust accurately. Models need to distinguish patients that incur high costs from patients that are sicker or have more medical needs and to distinguish the effects of variables such as cost, severity of illness, or medical need from effects of characteristics such as race. It is also important to be aware that in the healthc care domain in particular, the data that are available rarely reflect what we actually want to measure. That means that our measurements used for predicting and classifying are proxy measures. And when you use proxy measures, this introduces the possibility of systematic error or bias because proxies are always imperfect. And when the possibility of bias is reduced, there's always the possibility of discrimination, which is bias that leads to negative consequences for certain groups. It's important to recognize that the limitations of the models that are created when you lack data that are necessary to really explain the outcome of interest. For example, risk adjustment models that use health data typically do not include social factors such as income or education. These factors are well known to have strong effects on all sorts of health related outcomes including hospital readmissions. This example illustrates some specific features of AI model design that have e ethical implications and that could lead to harm to patients. Deep knowledge of the clinical characteristics of patients represented in data sets and familiarity with the limitations of available data are necessary to avoid ethical pitfalls in model design.

## 2. Ethical frameworks for health care and for AI.mp4

### Summary

This source explores the unique **ethical landscape of healthcare AI**, emphasizing that medical practice is governed by a **fiduciary responsibility** where the patient’s well-being must supersede personal or financial gain. Central to this framework is the **social contract of professional self-regulation**, which grants clinicians special powers and trust in exchange for adhering to strict codes of conduct. To maintain this trust, the text argues that AI development must be integrated into existing systems of **rigorous oversight and scientific validity**, ensuring these tools align with the professional duty to do no harm. Ultimately, the author suggests that understanding the **interplay of competing interests** within the health system is vital for ensuring that technological innovations remain focused on the **alignment of patient care and safety**.

**Keywords:** Healthcare ethical frameworks, Professional self-regulation, Fiduciary responsibilities, Artificial intelligence ethics, Conflict of interest

### Content

While healthcare can be thought of primarily in terms of financial transactions, it's important to remember that there are many ethical, legal, and regulatory factors that determine and constrain the financing and delivery of healthcare in ways that do not apply to other types of products and services. Right now, we'll be focusing on the broad ethical frameworks that are applicable to developing and using AI in the healthcare context. These include ethical frameworks that guide medicine, but also those that guide development and application of AI. First, all professionals are ethically bound and guided by professional ethics through codes and guidelines. These may not have the force of law behind them, but they do represent self-regulation that is the cornerstone of professional obligations and are critical to maintaining public trust. What do I mean by professional? I don't simply mean people who are paid for their services or people who have specialized expertise. By professional, I'm referring to those that may be paid for their work, but whose work serves a public interest. I'm including doctors, lawyers, and teachers as classic types of professionals. Professionals are entrusted with the well-being of people who seek their specialized expertise in times of need. Professionals have what is known as fiduciary responsibilities, meaning that they're expected to put the well-being of their clients ahead of their own interests. or the interests of others. In return, professionals are given broad and special powers in order to do their jobs. In the case of physicians, they're allowed access to their patients private health and financial information and are even allowed to do things that would be considered criminal in other contexts. However, the social contract that is implicit in the concept of professions allows such actions if they're necessary to serve the well-being of clients. For example, an anesthesiologist is allowed to rent render someone unconscious through the administration of drugs and a surgeon is allowed to then insert a knife into that person. When people who are not physicians do these sorts of things to others, they're usually considered criminals. However, it is assumed that clinical professionals are acting overall in the best interests of their patients, even if it at times it means that patients are subject to real harms. A key feature of professions is that they're given a lot of leeway to self-regulate. Medical professional association set standards for and implement programs of lensure of clinicians to practice medicine. They assure a minimum level of competence and provide oversight over medical misconduct. That's not to say that a professional can't be sued or go to jail for acting unprofessionally. It means that professionals get to define what is good and bad practice and who is permitted to engage in such practices. As I alluded to before, professional associations also set their own codes of ethics. So, I've said that professionals have fiduciary duties, meaning that they have ethical obligations to serve the best interests of their clients. It's important to note here that in order to serve the best interests of their patients, clinicians are extremely dependent on others to ensure that they are benefiting patients and not subjecting them to unnecessary harm. For example, clinicians have to rely on regulators and drug and device manufacturers to ensure that the diagnostics and treatments that they provide to patients are safe and effective. They have to rely on clinical researchers and scientists to provide valid evidence to support and guide clinical practices. The regulatory systems in place to evaluate drugs and devices and the certification systems to evaluate hospitals and laboratories are critical to maintaining the trust of both clinicians and patients. This becomes very important when thinking about how to develop artificial intelligence for healthcare applications because these systems need to be trusted in order to be implemented successfully. In addition, it is important to recognize that while clinicians and health care organizations are trying to serve the best interests of their patients, they have interests of their own and are subjected to many competing or conflicting interests. More importantly, in the context of healthcare delivery are financial interests. Understand Understanding the interplay of these interests and how they are shaped by the structure of the health system is critical to understanding how AI models can be aligned or misaligned with these interests and affect patient care and well-being.

## 4. More examples of AI and incentives in health care delivery and payment structures.mp4

### Summary

Health care systems increasingly utilize **artificial intelligence and predictive analytics** to respond to financial incentives designed to lower costs and improve patient outcomes. While programs like **bundled payments** and readmission penalties aim to encourage **efficient, high-quality care**, they can inadvertently drive providers to avoid high-risk patients or delay necessary treatments to protect their bottom line. These economic pressures create a risk that AI models will **exacerbate existing health disparities** if they are built on biased data or used to discriminate against vulnerable populations. Ultimately, creating **ethical and effective AI** requires a sophisticated understanding of how medical delivery and payment structures influence the behavior of health care providers.

**Keywords:** Predictive analytics, Hospital readmissions, Financial incentives, Bundled payments, Health disparities

### Content

Here's an example of a common application of AI. A large information technology company sells analytic services to health systems to integrate patients health data with insurance claims to help reduce inpatient admissions, emergency department admissions, and readmissions, which is when a patient who has been discharged from the hospital is admitted again within a certain time interval, such as 30 days. This example is just one of dozens, if not hundreds, hundreds of models that have been developed to predict hospital readmissions within 30 days of discharge. These predictive analytics have been developed in response to the Medicare hospital readmissions reduction program or HRRP which finds hospitals when they have excess readmissions within 30 days after adjustment for medical risks. This program applies to patients who are admitted to the hospital for a few specific conditions such as heart attacks or pneumonia. The program was designed to create financial incentives to reduce avoidable readmissions, which were thought to account for up to a third of all readmissions. There's evidence that the imposition of fines does in fact reduce readmissions. However, there's also evidence that the way that admissions are reduced might also lead to the unintended consequence of poorer or inappropriate care or no care at all. For example, some evidence suggested that hospitals reduce readmissions by retaining patients in observational units. Others have suggested that hospitals react to incentives by putting pressure on nursing homes not to send patients back within 30 days of discharge, which could delay necessary care. Hospitals can apply this pressure by not referring patients to nursing homes that lead to readmissions. Or patients could be discouraged from seeking timely care or even be less likely to be admitted in the first place if they're predicted to become a patient that is likely to be readmitted within 30 days. So, how information from a predictive model gets used in actual practice is also ethically relevant. Here's another example. A health system develops a model to predict costs of patients who have joint replacements. This application of AI would be developed in response to a type of incentive program to encourage valuebased care and is known as bundled payments. These programs focus on lowering costs of care. by paying multiple providers for what are called episodes of care for certain conditions. This is in contrast to a traditional fee for service payment program that pays individual providers for discrete services. So instead of paying a surgeon, an anesthesiologist, an orthopedist, and a physical therapist separately for a patient's joint replacement, payment would be bundled for all of this care and for the providers as a group. And they would manage the costs. If the costs of caring for the patient exceed the bundled payment, the providers share the burden. If the costs are lower than the payment, providers share what is left. This type of program is not as financially risky for providers as a fully capitated payment model, but it has more potential to control costs than the fully fee for service model. The intent of bundling payments for services included in an episode of care is to create incentives to improve patient outcomes. in transition from say the hospital to a rehab facility or a nursing home through better coordination of care. That is the intention is to align incentives of providers across a series of healthcare settings. However, hospitals and providers still have an incentive to lower costs and therefore identify patients who are going to incur either low or high costs. And as for the examples we just saw, there are existing health disparities could interact with the bundled payment programs to exacerbate these health disparities. For example, there's evidence that even though African-Americans are disproportionately affected by arthritis, they are less likely to receive joint replacements than white patients. There's also evidence that African-Americans are more likely to be discharged after joint replacements to facilities that lead to 30-day hospital readmissions. And again, if predictive models do not include the right data and do not risk adjust appropriately, health disparities could be reinforced. These are the main takeaway points. First, health care systems deliberately use incentives to encourage appropriate and efficient use of health care resources through improving quality and lowering costs of care. These incentives make it useful to predict risk, especially unexpected financial and medical risk. And AI can be useful for improving the accuracy of these kinds of predictive analytics. Incentives can have unintended consequences which can lead to poorer care or discrimination against vulnerable groups or exacerbation of existing health disparities. It is important to understand and anticipate the impacts of proxy measures and the limits of health data on bias in models. It's also important to understand how behavior of those who use the output of models could be affected in ways that could create unintended consequences. In short, fairly Sophisticated and detailed knowledge of how health care services are delivered and paid for are necessary to understand how to build good and ethical AI models.

## 5. Overview of AI applications in delivery of health care services and ethical issues.mp4

### Summary

As modern medicine generates vast quantities of digital data, artificial intelligence has emerged as a transformative tool for improving **diagnostics, surgical precision, and personalized treatment plans**. However, the integration of these technologies into healthcare delivery introduces profound **ethical challenges**, specifically regarding whether these tools truly benefit patients or exacerbate **social injustices**. Key concerns include the **privacy of sensitive medical information**, the potential for **algorithmic bias** to disadvantage marginalized groups, and a pervasive **lack of transparency** in how complex models reach life-altering conclusions. Ultimately, because clinicians remain legally and morally responsible for patient outcomes, there is an urgent need for **rigorous standards and a deep understanding of AI limitations** to navigate the competing interests inherent in the healthcare industry.

**Keywords:** Healthcare data analysis, AI diagnostic accuracy, Ethical implications, Data bias concerns, Transparency and safety

### Content

There are many parts of a modern health care system, each of which collects and analyzes data. Health care providers and intermediaries such as health insurance companies, government programs such as Medicare and Medicaid generate huge amounts of data that patients can tell you something about the care that they receive, their health status, outcomes of their care, and the costs that are associated with that care. Almost all of these data are now in digital form and some of it usable for analysis through artificial intelligence. There are wide range of applications in artificial intelligence to healthcare, including increasing the accuracy of diagnosis, robotic surgeries, identifying candidates for drug development, or determining the best treatments to use for specific patients. Like any tool or innovation, however, artificial intelligence raises ethical issues that could be considered by its developers and users and important stakeholders such as patients. I'll be highlighting aspects of the health care system that I think are important to think about if you are a user or developer of AI systems and drawing attention to their ethical implications. I'll be focusing here on a particular subset of applications of artificial intelligence most related to financing and delivery of health care services. But what are the ethical issues? There are many, but broadly speaking, the questions we should ask are first whether artificial intelligence tools help or harm patients. their families and caregivers or healthcare providers and second whether these tools are socially just or not. The ethical issues can arise from different aspects of AI such as the nature of the data that are used, the way that data are collected, the way that AI models are designed or how their output is interpreted and used. In addition, there can be broad social implications to the extent that AI systems are intended to replace humans at healthcare related jobs. You might have heard about some of the general ethical concerns raised by artificial intelligence. A big concern is about the privacy and security of digital data, especially in the domain of healthcare because healthcare data are sensitive. In addition, there are many laws and regulations specific to the use and transmission of healthcare data. Computer scientists and journalists have discovered ways that AI can lead to conclusions that are biased. That is based on systematic error because their models are trained on data. that are unrepresentative of populations or features of interest. Systematic error in AI models is especially bad in the healthcare context since output of these models can affect important and even life and death decisions. Sometimes these systematic errors can lead to unfair decisions, especially if they discriminate against whole categories of socially disadvantaged people such as racial minorities, women, children, or people with low income. One type of ethical concern that is particularly relevant to AI is due to the lack of transparency of AI models. It's often difficult or even impossible to tell how AI arrives at its conclusions, especially if the AI uses machine learning techniques that mean that the models are constantly changing based on the data on which they're operating. This is of particular concern in the healthcare context because clinicians and healthcare organizations s are reliant on AI developers to provide tools and technologies that are safe and effective to use on their patients. But currently there are few standards or regulations for evaluating safety and effectiveness of many AI based products used in healthcare. Yet clinicians and health care providers are ethically and legally responsible for the decisions that are increasingly informed by AI. So it's critically important for clinicians and health system that use AI in ways that can influence health care decisions to understand the limitations of the AI techniques, data, and models as they are applied to their specific patient populations. One type of ethical concern that we'll focus on in this course is the issue of conflicting or competing interests. This is an issue that arises particularly in the domain of healthcare.
