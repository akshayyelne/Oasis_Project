## 1. AI and The Learning Health System.mp4

### Summary

The source explores the concept of the **learning health system (LHS)** as a solution to the ethical and regulatory hurdles facing **artificial intelligence** and medical research. It argues that traditional ethics, which strictly separate **clinical practice from research**, are often poorly suited for a system where data collection and **continual improvement in care** are seamlessly integrated. By proposing a new ethical framework that includes **obligations for both providers and patients**, the text suggests that contributing to medical knowledge should be viewed as a **shared responsibility** rather than a burdensome regulatory hurdle. Ultimately, the author calls for a shift toward **transparency and accountability** over rigid informed consent, urging oversight bodies to utilize the **existing flexibility** in regulations to better support systematic learning.

**Keywords:** Learning health systems, Artificial intelligence research, Research ethics frameworks, Informed consent challenges, Medical practice research

### Content

We will see that a potential solution to the problems presented by regulations governing human subjects research can be found in the concept of the learning health system. In particular, we have seen that artificial intelligence research and in particular the data collection needed for research presents a difficult trade-off between fully informed consent on the one hand and the ability to produce valid representative scientifically and medically important research. For secondary uses of data collected for clinical or public purposes, it can sometimes be a challenge to obtain meaningful consent at all. For uses of data in research repositories, it will typically not be possible to obtain consent for each use of the data, requiring a broad consent rather than anything approximating fully informed consent. The fact that findings from this research may sometimes be returned, makes the consent issues even more complicated. The starting point for modern research ethics, including the Belmont report, is the distinction between research and practice. This distinction usually makes sense for research aimed at develop ing new treatments for patients which is typically conducted in clinical trials. But some research is not aimed at trying to develop new drugs or interventions but instead aims to improve different aspects of medical practice. For example, sometimes research is done to find out which of two interventions that are within the range of usual practice is better. Sometimes research-like work is done through quality improvement efforts to find ways to deliver care better in a particular setting. Sometimes research is done to find out which of several different commonly used interventions for a particular condition works best for a particular subgroup of patients or maybe even for an individual patient. AI research has the potential to help address all of these issues. My colleagues and I refer to this type of research as romp or research on medical practices. When an institution makes a commitment to systematically carry out romp using all of its data and clinical practices as a way of improving care, it is sometimes called the learning health system. In 2007, the Institute of medicine, now called the National Academy of Medicine, defined the learning healthcare system as quote, "one in which knowledge generation is so embedded into the core of the practice of medicine that it is a natural outgrowth and product of the healthcare delivery process and leads to continual improvement in care." Bioethsists working on the learning health system have challenged the distinction between research and practice. In a learning health system, every patient is also a research participant and their data represent an opportunity to learn. The primary goal of clinical care is to benefit the patient. But setting up systems to make use of that data in systematic ways can both benefit patients and create opportunities to learn and improve. Thus, the learning health system is a challenge to the traditional line drawn between clinical practice on the one hand and research on the other. A learning health care system requires a feedback loop between clinical practice and new knowledge and also a feedback loop between that knowledge and improving patient care. Care creates new evidence that leads to improved care that creates new evidence. To achieve this, clinical practice has to be set up to allow for the creation of knowledge, but also for emerging evidence to alter care. Given the large and diverse nature of the data achieved learning health system, artificial intelligence is almost certainly likely to play a critical role both in the learning process from the data and then in the development of methods to translate new knowledge into altered practice. Critics of applying regulatory frameworks that were designed for protecting participants in clinical trials using unproven interventions and applying those standards to research in a learning health system worry that it is overly burdensome and creates disincentives to learn. As a result, it overprotects participants from minimal risk activities while missing opportunities to avoid problems and errors that commonly occur in clinical practice. Ruth Feden, Nancy Cass, and other critics of the current research ethics paradigm derived from the development report have developed an alternative ethical framework for the learning health system. Their framework is based on recognition of a set of obligations or duties held by different stakeholders in the learning health system. Though they frame them as a contrast to the Belmont report, the seven obligations they identify can be seen as actually elaborations of the Belmont report which they have at times acknowledged. So the first duty or obligation is respect for persons. That's the first principle of the Belmont report. This is captured in the learning health system by the duty to respect the rights and dignity of the patients in the new framework. The second Belmont principle of beneficence is captured in the new health system framework with three obligations. The obligation to provide optimal care for each patient, the duty to avoid imposing nonclinical risks and burdens, and the obligation to improve clinical care through learning. The third Belmont principle of justice is cashed out in the learning health system as the duty to address and reduce unjust inequalities. And that's the way this framework elaborates the Belar principles. In addition to these five duties, there are two obligations that are not covered in the BMAR report. The duty to respect clinical judgment is new and reflects changes in medical practice since Obama report was written. Increasingly, physicians are losing the ability to autonomously make decisions for their patients. Clinical pathways and formulary decisions and insurance coverage are all impinging on the freedom of clinicians to make decisions. Sometimes these restrictions on freedom lead to improvements in patient outcomes. Assessing the impact of a system on clinical judgment requires placing it in the context of existing clinical limitations that are seen as appropriate as well as on the evidence basis of the decision-m by the clinician. These six obligations that we've discussed so far, the five that fell under the Belmont report as well as this new sixth obligation all fall on researchers, clinicians, healthcare administrators, payers, and purchasers. But there's a seventh duty in the framework for the learning health system and it's maybe the most significant addition to the Belmont report and that's the recognition that patients may have obligations or duties to contribute to knowledge particularly in situations or there's little to no risk to them. Patients have a duty to contribute to the learning system that they themselves benefit from. It is worth noting that this is a departure from the ethical framework developed by Hans Yonas that was very influential at the time that the Belmont was report was written. He argued except under dire circumstances for example during a plague patients have no obligation to participate in research. Yonas seems to have had in mind research that involves risk or sacrifice by the research participant not learning activities that are often hard to distinguish from required quality assurance activities that are actually necessary to provide adequate care. Just as the Belmont report does not explicitly offer a system for balancing potentially conflicting principles, so too this framework does not offer details about how to balance the trade-offs that exist between these seven obligations. Does the obligation to respect the rights and dignity of patients require informed consent requirements that are incompatible with the other obligations of the learning health system, particularly the duties of beneficence and justice? It is worth pointing out that informed consent and respect for patients is a core aspect of the ethics of clinical care, not just research. But research regulations governing consent for research have become very cumbersome in ways that clinical informed consent, which is often framed as a conversation between a patient and their physician have not. So another way of framing this is how best to balance respect for patients and the other obligations of a learning health system. Is a complicated consent form to disclose minimal risk activities that improve patient care really the best way to demonstrate respect? And while we have seen that there is data showing that patients want to consent for the uses of their data, there's also evidence that they want the fruits of the research. They want learning to happen. They want healthcare to improve. Studying the trade-offs between the two is very challenging. But in a study my colleagues and I conducted, we found that the vast majority of patients and members of the public were willing to accept less elaborated consent approaches if it were necessary for valuable comparative effectiveness research to take place. In addition, there's evidence that most patients want their physicians to tell them about these sorts of activities as part of their care rather than as a separate research related engagement. Feden and Cass have suggested that there may be better ways of demonstrating respect for either patients or research participants than the standard informed consent forms. In particular, active engagement with patients about ongoing learning activities so that this becomes an expectation of patients, transparency about the kinds of learning that are taking place and especially accountability in translating the results to improve patient care. So that last loop actually winds up taking place are all different ways to demonstrate respect for patients and research participants. It is worth noting that something like this already takes place, but it's in regard to individual learning rather than systems learning. Teaching hospitals require that patients receive some of their care from trainees as a learning activity. Every clinician has to learn at some point. So patients have a duty to receive care from trainees in exchange for the benefits of being at a teaching hospital. This is not something that patients can choose not to participate in. Hence, there is no consent, but it's something that most patients are familiar with and there is transparency about the fact of training involvement and accountability to minimize risks and harms to patients and produce adequately trained physicians. An analogous approach to system learning is needed. Just like we already have a system for individual learning, we need a system for learning as a system. And the research regulations and the Belma report are flexible enough to accommodate this as long as IRBs or research ethics committees are willing to utilize appropriate mechanisms such as waiver or alteration of consent when it is appropriate. Unfortunately, some of our research has indicated that IRBs are often unwilling to utilize those mechanisms. So, more education of IRBs so that they take advantage of the flexibility that actually exists in our current system can can take place.

## 3. Ethical Issues in Data sources for AI.mp4

### Summary

The development of sophisticated AI tools in healthcare relies on massive datasets sourced from **dedicated research repositories**, repurposed **clinical records**, or **unregulated consumer devices**. While these data pools enable precision medicine, they raise significant ethical hurdles regarding **informed consent**, as traditional disclosure is often replaced by **broad consent** to allow for future, unforeseen scientific studies. To protect participants, institutions must balance data utility with robust **security measures** and clear communication regarding privacy risks and potential commercial profits. Ultimately, the source emphasizes that achieving **justice and equity** in AI requires active efforts to include marginalized groups and repair historical mistrust, ensuring that the benefits of technological advancement are distributed fairly across all populations.

**Keywords:** AI research data, Research repositories, Informed consent, Data privacy, Justice and equity

### Content

AI research can take many forms. Researchers use deep learning to try and identify and understand novel pharmaccogenomic variants. Others develop novel algorithms or models to better predict which patients will benefit from aggressive palative radiation. Researchers have developed tools to improve identification of tumors and radiological images. Other tools have been developed to improve health insurance claims processing. But in order for those new tools to be developed, AI requires data for it to learn from. In some cases, it can require large quantities of data. AI has often been linked to the concepts of big data and precision health. The vision of creating more precisely tailored interventions for individual patients requires that large amounts of data from different sources be used to understand why different patients respond differently to the same medications and why different patients have different side effect profiles. Broadly speaking, there are three overlapping types of data for AI research. First, data can come from research repositories that are explicitly created for research purposes. So these projects have as their primary goal creation of a tool to be used and creation of data to be used for research. Projects like the National Institute of Health precision medicine initiative, all of us research program or the UK 100,000 genomes project are illustrations of research repositories. While these projects often include data from electronic health records and from direct to consumer wearables, these research repositories also include samples collected for research surveys and explicit permission to use other data. So the idea there is these are research repositories that were explicitly created for the purpose of research. A second data source commonly used for AI research is the secondary use of research that's collected for other purposes. This could include electronic health record data which is collected from patients as just part of the routine clinical care data collected for public repositories such as newborn blood spots or census data and administrative data such as health claim records. A third data source for AI research is the information collected outside of either healthcare or research through consumer information. This could include most wearable and mobile health data, things like my watch, direct to consumer genetics, computer usage data. The important thing to note about this last source of data is that it is typically outside of the normally uh uh regulated research system of medical and research um activities. Instead, it's being done by companies outside of those things. So, the mechanisms governing oversight don't apply. Most companies that collect this information don't have requirements for protection of human subjects. They don't have the privacy requirements that might be met within the health system unless they happen to collaborate with medical researchers or institutions that fall within those systems or if they seek uh approval from the FDA for what they're doing. Data that has been collected for a research repository such as a bioank has generally been consented at the time of of a data collection since it is a primary research activity and involves interaction with the persons donating the material. Therefore, creation of a research repository is generally regarded as research on human subjects and is therefore subject to regulatory requirements and to the ethical requirements outlined in the Belmont report. This includes the requirement to obtain informed consent. The first major challenge for informed consent in this type of research is whether each use of data requires a specific consent or whether you could have an open-ended or broad consent. There are also approaches that use a tiered consent process where individuals consent to broad categories of their data use. While specific discussion of the benefits, risks, and purposes of each use of someone's data seems like it would be required for a true informed consent, it would also render most research repositories completely unusable because you can't anticipate all the possible future uses of research. As a result, the recently revised common rule, that is the regulations that govern research on human subjects, largely based on the Belmont report, explicitly allow broad consent in place of a true informed consent. As we have seen the Belma report anticipated that incomplete disclosure will sometimes be necessary and acceptable when a waiver is truly required for some types of research. However, there are a number of regulatory requirements for broad consent to be utilized. Participants still need to be told of any known risks. For example, risks associated with sample collection such as a blood draw or risks to privacy of the data that's being collected. Similarly, any anticipated benefits have to be disclosed along with details about how confidentiality of information is to be maintained as well as that participation is voluntary and if there are any plans to do genetic analysis. In addition, participants should be told whether their data may be used to generate profit and if there is, whether there will be any sharing of that profit. Some effort should be made to convey at least some sense of the type of research that will take place with their specimens, what types of data will be used, how long the data will be stored and available for research uses, and they should typically be told that they will not be informed of all possible future uses. In addition to the of informed consent. Research repositories require careful security efforts and provisions to provide for the privacy of the information that is entrusted to them. This ensures that risk to participants are minimized as much as possible and that the benefits of research with the repository are likely going to outweigh the risks. Finally, a major concern with research repositories involves considerations of justice. Research repositories have generally recruited individuals who are non-Hispanic white, middle class or affluent, and generally well educated. This is is an excellent illustration of the distinction between a fair process and a fair outcome. Even if the data collection process in creating these bio banks is done in a fair and equitable manner, for example, by not excluding members of any group and maybe even by trying to recruit individuals from marginalized communities, there can still be an unjust distribution due to the underlying social and racial inequalities. So, you may have a fair process, but an unequal distribution. The history of mistrust by many members of underrepresented groups such as African- Americans can be traced to a history of mistrust. This mistrust has been earned by a biomedical system that has historically discriminated against certain populations. Therefore, creating fair access to the results of artificial intelligence research will require great effort to repair trust in these populations, to build relationships between underrepresented communities and researchers, and to succeed in improving recruitment and engagement. There is some promising improvement in some of these efforts, but it remains an open question whether research repositories can ever be truly representative of the public.

## 4. Ethics Summary.mp4

### Summary

Ethical artificial intelligence research is built upon the foundational pillars of **respect for persons, beneficence, and justice**, yet applying these ideals to modern data collection remains a complex challenge. While traditional research relies on **informed consent**, the use of secondary medical records and private consumer data often makes obtaining explicit permission impractical, creating a difficult **trade-off between individual autonomy and the collective benefits** of large-scale analysis. Furthermore, strict consent barriers risk excluding marginalized groups, which threatens the **equitable distribution of research benefits** across diverse populations. To navigate these tensions, the text proposes a **learning healthcare system** model, where data sharing is treated as a transparent, mutual duty. This framework ensures that patient contributions are balanced by **institutional accountability**, guaranteeing that any insights gained are directly translated into **improved clinical care** for the community.

**Keywords:** Artificial intelligence ethics, Belmont Report principles, Informed consent challenges, Data source variety, Learning healthcare systems

### Content

There are many ethical issues raised by artificial intelligence. In this module, we focused on the broad ethical framework that governs artificial intelligence research and the ethical issues raised by the uses of data from different sources. The Belmont report presents the three basic principles that govern research ethics. Respect for persons which includes respect for patient autonomy or the ability to make decisions for oneself. Beneficence, the requirement to maximize benefit and minimize harm to participants. And justice, the requirement that the allocation of the benefits and risks of research participation will be fairly distributed. The different sources of data that can be used for AI research raise different ethical issues. Some data comes from repositories that were specifically created for research. Some data are available for secondary use after they were collected for other purposes such as electronic health record data that was primarily collected as a part of repeat clinical care. Finally, some data are collected outside of the healthare system entirely largely by private companies that directly engage with people for example through their devices like their phones, their computers, or their smartwatches. Normally, respect for persons and their autonomy requires voluntary informed consent for the research participation. This makes sense for most clinical trials, but it's much more challenging in the context of data available for artificial intelligence research. It is not possible to say in advance what future uses researchers will make of research repositories. Therefore, most research repositories only have a broad consent about future uses of participants data. And for secondary uses of data collected, for other purposes and may be impractical to obtain consent at all without significant loss of data. Finally, most direct to consumer data that is made available to researchers arises in a context of minimal regulation. For users of mobile health technologies through their smartphones or wearable technologies such as smartwatches, it is definitely a situation of buyer beware. There can be a trade-off between the value of research that can be done with large amounts of data from these sources and voluntary fully informed consent. So between the value of the re on the one hand and consent on the other. Most people want research to be done, but they also want to be asked permission for the uses of their data and research. In addition, the greater the consent barrier, the more likely the data are to underrepresent populations that have traditionally been underrepresented in research, such as poor people as well as racial and ethnic minorities. Thus, there's a trade-off between respect for persons on the one hand and beneficence and justice on the other. How should these trade-offs be managed? We've seen that the learning healthare system offers one way of balancing these conflicting duties. The learning health system accepts that patients getting health care have a duty to contribute to future learning as long as certain key conditions are met. The keys to a robust learning health system are that there is active engagement of patients, that there's transparency about the facts that the data is being used for learning as well as accountability. This last point, accountability is critical. If artificial intelligence is successful in learning about how to improve improve healthcare for an individual patient or strata of patients that learning has to be translated into improved care. Just as patients know that if they go to a teaching hospital, teaching will take place, patients need to understand if they go to a learning health system, learning will take place.

## 6. Introduction to Research Ethics and AI.mp4

### Summary

The ethical integration of artificial intelligence into medicine relies on a fundamental **distinction between research**, which aims to produce generalizable knowledge, and **clinical practice**, which focuses on individual patient benefit. Historically, this boundary became essential after several high-profile **ethical scandals** revealed a lack of informed consent and the mistreatment of vulnerable subjects. In response, frameworks like the **Belmont Report** established a regulatory foundation built upon three core pillars: **respect for persons**, which honors autonomy; **beneficence**, which requires maximizing benefits while minimizing harm; and **justice**, which ensures a fair distribution of research burdens. These principles remain the essential guide for ensuring that the development and validation of **new AI tools** remain ethically sound and protective of human rights.

**Keywords:** Research vs Practice, Artificial Intelligence Healthcare, Ethical Research Principles, Human Subject Protection, Belmont Report Guidelines

### Content

Any discussion of the role of artificial intelligence in healthcare has to carefully distinguish the development of models, algorithms, predictive tools, and so on from the routine uses of those tools in medical practice, whether as a decision aid or as a screening tool. The development of AI is a research activity to identify, develop, and refine tools that may later be useful in providing healthcare. Once particular applications of AI have been validated or found to be acceptable to healthcare providers or payers or systems, the uses of those applications are part of clinical practice. ice rather than research. So that's the important distinction to know, the distinction between research and practice. Research is an activity that is usually structured and has as its primary goal the production of generalizable knowledge. It's often designed to generate or test hypothesis and generally to produce knowledge or develop new interventions. The individuals who are the subjects of research are referred to as research participants while the individuals who conduct the research are researchers. In contrast, clinical practice is an activity that aims to benefit individual patients. So, it's supposed to make people get better either to help make a diagnosis, prevent disease, provide treatment to them. All these things are tried to help benefit patients. There should be a reasonable expectation of success in the clinical practice that's being undertaken. We'll eventually see ways in which this distinction can be challenging, but a great deal of the modern history of research ethics, as well as most regulatory systems draws a fairly sharp line between research on the one hand and clinical practice on the other. This foundry was drawn in response to a series of scandals and revelations in the 1960s and '7s that raised concerns about the ability of clinical researchers to self-regulate. The Tuskegee Civilist study, a natural history in which African-American men were denied antibiotics and were misled into thinking that lumbar punctures and other data collection measures were treatments for bad blood, was the most infamous of several problematic studies. In 1966, Harvard anesthesiologist and bioeththics pioneer Henry Beecher published a seminal paper in the New England Journal of Medicine that showed that most published clinical trials had made no attempt to obtain informed consent from research participants. Many trials at the time were conducted by clinicians who studied their patients with no consent and with the roles of researcher and clinician blurred. As a result of these scandals, new guidance for the conduct of research on human subjects was developed. The Nermberg code which had been written in 1947 as part of the aftermath of revelations of unethical Nazi experimentation had clearly not been suff efficient. Given the findings of Beecher and others, it's clear that more needed to be done. The Declaration of Helsinki, first published in 1964, and it's been revised several times since then, became the first major update and included a requirement for independent review of formal research protocols. Many countries have adopted Helsinki as their regulations for research on human subjects. But here in the United States, we did something a little different. In 1974, the National Research Act created a national commission for the protection of human subjects of biomedical and behavioral research. The final report of the commission became known as the famous Belmont Report and was issued in 1978 and published in the Federal Register in 1979. While there was significant overlap with Helsinki, particularly regarding the concrete recommendations about how to regulate human subjects research, the Belmont report included a very influential ethical framework that provided a foundation for those recommendations. In 1981, the US adopted a regulatory framework largely out of those recommendations and the Belmar continues to be the basis of research and the regulations that apply today. The Belmont report articulates three ethical principles that should govern the protection of human subjects research. Others later attempted to show that these same principles play an important role in the ethical conduct of clinical practice as well. The first ethical principle articulated in the Bmont report is respect for persons. The principle or duty can be broken out into two components. The first is to respect the autonomy of agents who have the capacity to make their own decisions and the second and is to protect individuals with diminished capacity. The second principle of the Belmont report is beneficence. This obligation can also be broken down into two components. First is a fundamental principle of medical ethics that's been widely seen as applicable to biomedical research. Primosir or do no harm. More particularly the second component is that possible benefits should be maximized and the possible harms of research should be minimized as much as possible. The third principle is justice. This requires that there be a fair disc distribution of the benefits and burdens of research. You don't want one group of individuals being burdened with research for the benefit of others in an unfair fashion. Aristotle's formal principle of justice requires that similar cases should be treated similarly or put differently that if two cases or individuals are to be treated differently, there needs to be a relevant difference between them. Together, these principles and their application are the keys to understanding the requirements for ethical development of new AI tools.

## 8. Secondary Uses of Data.mp4

### Summary

Modern AI research frequently relies on the **secondary use of data**, which involves analyzing information like medical records or genetic samples originally gathered for clinical care or public health rather than research. Because obtaining **informed consent** for these massive datasets is often logistically impossible, institutions navigate three regulatory pathways: classifying the work as **quality assurance**, using **de-identified data** stripped of personal markers, or securing a **formal waiver** based on minimal risk. However, the rise of artificial intelligence has created an ethical crisis by demonstrating that **re-identification** of anonymized data is increasingly possible, challenging the notion that such data is no longer tied to an individual's identity. This creates a profound tension between the **ethical duty to respect autonomy** through consent and the practical need for **diverse, representative datasets** that are necessary to improve healthcare for all populations.

**Keywords:** Secondary data use, Informed consent, Data deidentification, AI reidentification risks, Regulatory compliance pathways

### Content

A lot of AI research makes use of data that was collected for purposes other than research. In addition to clinical data, the billing claims sent from the patients healthcare system or physician or hospital get sent to an insurance company to create a warehouse of insurance. As you have seen, this data has the potential to be very valuable for data mining or other applications of artificial intelligence. ## 9. The Belmont Report A Framework for Research Ethics.mp4

### Summary

The Belmont Report provides a foundational ethical framework for human research, structured around three core pillars: **respect for persons**, **beneficence**, and **justice**. To honor human dignity, researchers must prioritize **voluntary informed consent**, ensuring that autonomous individuals understand the risks while providing extra protections for **vulnerable populations** with diminished capacity. The principle of beneficence mandates a rigorous **risk-benefit analysis**, requiring scientists to minimize potential harms while maximizing the social and individual gains of their study. Finally, the pursuit of justice ensures a **fair distribution** of research burdens and rewards, guarding against systemic biases that might exploit specific social or racial groups. Ultimately, these guidelines serve as the essential criteria for **institutional review boards** to evaluate the morality of modern scientific endeavors, including the rapidly evolving field of **artificial intelligence**.

**Keywords:** Belmont Report Principles, Informed Consent, Respect For Persons, Principle Of Beneficence, Justice In Research

### Content

The three principles that are articulated in the Belmont report are the framework for the regulatory system governing human subjects research and have been enormously influential in understanding the ethics of research on human subjects. Research to develop new AI tools or to develop new knowledge or findings from human data will fall under these regulations and should be broadly consistent with this ethical framework. The first principle respect for persons includes the duty to treat individuals as autonomous agents. Autonomy is at its core the ility of individuals to self-legislate in light of reason.
