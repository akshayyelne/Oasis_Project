Instead of billing for every individual service, an algorithm known as a **grouper** assigns patients to categories with assigned **weights** that reflect the average cost and complexity of treating their specific diagnosis. This structure incentivizes efficiency because the reimbursement remains the same regardless of the length of stay or the volume of resources used. While modern versions have evolved to include **outlier payments** for exceptionally expensive cases and adjustments for major surgical procedures, the core purpose of the DRG remains to move away from **fee-for-service** models toward a more standardized, predictable method of global hospital financing.

### Summary

The concept of **pay for performance**, or P4P, serves as a financial strategy in healthcare designed to link provider compensation directly to the **achievement of quality-based goals**. These models utilize **clinical process and outcome measures** to determine whether medical professionals receive monetary bonuses or face penalties based on their ability to meet specific benchmarks. While the approach aims to elevate the standard of care, it introduces complex challenges regarding **how to select fair metrics** and adjust for the inherent difficulty of treating diverse patient populations. Ultimately, the effectiveness of P4P depends on **balancing financial stakes** to ensure providers are motivated to improve without distorting their clinical focus or risking their financial stability.

## 13. Risk and Incentives in Hospital Payment.mp4

### Summary

Hospital payment models exist on a spectrum that determines how much **financial risk** is shifted from the payer to the provider, ranging from low-risk fee-for-service models to high-risk **global budgets**. As this risk increases, the **behavioral incentives** for hospitals shift from potentially over-delivering care to maximize revenue toward minimizing services and shortening stays to preserve a fixed budget. This creates a delicate **quality trade-off** between the dangers of medical overuse in flexible systems and the threat of under-provision in capped systems. Ultimately, managing these incentives effectively requires **aligning physician actions** with administrative goals and implementing robust monitoring to ensure that financial efficiency does not compromise patient outcomes.

**Keywords:** Hospital payment systems, Financial risk transfer, Healthcare delivery incentives, Service use trade-offs, Physician incentive alignment

### Content

The related topics of risk and incentives often come up when we think about hospital payment systems. Let's take a look. First, risk. Hospital payment systems can move risk around. We can think of a continuum here. On one end, fee for service systems transfer the least or maybe no risk from intermediaries to hospitals. The intermediaries keep the risk associated with patients needing more or less medical care. PEDDM systems transfer some risk. Here, hospitals would not be at risk when more people get sick or need hospital care or when patients need to stay longer because they get paid every time more people get sick or every time people stay longer. The intermediary keeps that risk, but the fixed payment per day would leave the hospital on the hook for how much care gets delivered in the day. If patients get a lot of care and that costs the hospital a lot, they'd have the responsibility for that. DRG systems transfer even a bit more risk. Here, the hospital gets a fixed payment per discharge. So now When a patient needs more days in the hospital or when they need more services per day, the hospital has to bear the costs of that. Outlier payment components might help with this, but they probably don't entirely eliminate the risk. Global budgets would transfer the most risk. Here, the hospital's got a fixed budget and accepts the risk associated with whether people get sick or not and the variations in the amount of care they're going to need. Related, though a little different, is the topic of incentives. Hospital payment systems, like any payment system, create some incentives and we can think about how these incentives might affect healthcare delivery. Fee for service would tend to create incentives to perform more services which generate more revenue for the hospital. PDM systems similarly would tend to create incentives for hospitals to keep patients in the hospital longer. You might think some hospitals would take these incentives very seriously and respond strongly and that indeed can happen. But it's often more fair to think of this in a more subtle way. I think there might be reasons that would tend toward patients staying in the hospital longer and fee for service or PDMs simply allow that to go on and provide no reason to try and shorten hospital stays as they creep to include more and more services and longer times. When there's a decision that could go either way, they probably skew toward longer or more services. This just enables in the long run hospital stays to get longer and more services to be provided. DRG systems on the other hand create incentives to keep patients in the hospital less time, shorter hospital hospital stays. Once you pay the fixed amount per admission to the hospital, the hospital then benefits if they can get that patient out as soon as possible and get another patient in because the new admission will generate a new DRG payment. Global budgets would provide the strongest incentives to minimize the amount of care, the amount of work that gets done for each patient. From a quality and resource use perspective, we can get some trade-offs toward the fee for service and the predium end of the spectrum. We worry less about under use of care, which might be good, but we might worry about creating overuse, patients staying in the hospital longer than they need to, for example. On the other end of the spectrum, the opposite. We steer toward reduced overuse, but we start to risk underuse, patients getting kicked out before they should be. An important issue related to incentives for hospitals stems from the fact that hospitals and physicians are often in separate organizations, but physicians are in control of a lot of things that happen in the hospital. Which treatment patients get, how long they stay, those can be strongly affected by physicians. Maybe you can take a fee for service or perdeium system, but add other measurement or monitoring to try and minimize overuse of services. Maybe you can nudge hospitals in other ways to keep patients from staying too long.
