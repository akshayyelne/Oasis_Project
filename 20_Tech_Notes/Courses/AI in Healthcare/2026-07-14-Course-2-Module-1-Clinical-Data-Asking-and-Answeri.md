## 2. Example making decision to treat.mp4

### Summary

This text explores the transition from analyzing historical data to making active **clinical decisions** for an individual patient. It highlights how practitioners often substitute a patient’s specific needs with a **descriptive statistical question**, assuming that past data accurately predicts future individual outcomes. By categorizing patients through **risk stratification**, doctors can determine whether a case requires aggressive intervention, such as anti-coagulation. Ultimately, the process serves as a framework for **balancing potential benefits against the risks** of treatment side effects to ensure a well-rounded medical conclusion.

**Keywords:** Clinical decision making, Risk stratification, Patient proportions, Treatment outcomes, Adverse event risks

### Content

Now that we've had this discussion, what do you think about our analysis for Laura? Note that the question we asked was about treating Laura who was experiencing a set of clinical conditions. However, the question we answered was about the proportion of patients with a set of conditions who developed a blood clot. So, what we answered was a descriptive question relying on counts and proportions and maybe their ratios. We then need to make an assumption that what happened in the past to these patients is likely to happen to Laura as as well. That assumption and then the resulting conclusion provides us with a risk stratification i.e. groups our patients into a high-risisk or a low-risk category. If we conclude that Laura is in the high-risisk category, what treatment to offer is clear, which is to use anti-coagulation. In real life, we would also need to draw similar conclusions about the risks of adverse events resulting from the treatment itself before we can make a final decision.

## 3. Finding similr patients.mp4

### Summary

To effectively identify specific cohorts within electronic medical records, researchers must **transform raw data into a structured format** that aligns with their clinical objectives. This process relies heavily on **medical expertise** to select accurate diagnostic codes and keywords, especially when critical information is hidden within unstructured documents. By utilizing **proxy markers**—such as a specific medication used as a substitute for a missing lab result—investigators can bridge gaps in available data. Ultimately, combining clinical knowledge with strategic queries provides a **faster and more reliable path** to defining patient groups and tracking health outcomes than relying on purely automated methods.

**Keywords:** Electronic medical records, Patient similarity criteria, Medical data transformation, Clinical domain expertise, Proxy marker identification

### Content

We need to extract and transform the data from the electronic medical record into a form that we can use to answer our question. We want to find patients meeting the criteria stated in a research question. However, because EMR data are not necessarily organized in a way that makes such searches straightforward, we need some medical expertise to choose the diagnosis codes and the terms that can identify patients that are in a similar situation. So, let's review the steps we would need to take. First, we need to find all pediatric patients in the medical record system. This can be done using a query based on patient age of all the patients that are children or under 18. We retain only those with a diagnosis code of SLE. We can find patients with protein ura by checking on the values listed in a urine test. How do we find patients with antifphospholipid antibodies? Ideally, this laboratory test would be recorded in numeric form. However, often the result is pasted into a textual document or could be scanned report and a PDF. We could resort to some fancy computation or we could rely on some domain expertise. So for example in this case we can use treatment with aspirin as a proxy marker and we can do this because we know that patients with a positive test for this condition are extremely likely to be treated with aspirin. We can confirm the results of that search by checking laboratory test results for those antibodies for the patients who have the results in a searchable form. Now we're in the midst of step three. We've defined our criteria that allow us to identify the appropriate group of similar patients. Our outcome of interest is clotting and we need to know if any of these patients developed a blood clot. We'll search for thrombosis or blood clot. Again, relying on our clinical expertise to identify those terms. Note that this step does require knowing some medicine. While it's tempting to automate this and it perhaps can be done with enough resources, using what we know about medical care offers us a faster path.

 Copyright © Stanford University Dealing with missing values One widely used method is to impute the missing values. Imputation is a kind of prediction that fills in the missing values based on other information in the dataset. A simple imputation method, called column mean imputation, replaces the missing value with the mean of the known values in the same column. This assumes that the variable’s values in the other rows of that column have information about the missing value, which is often not true in medicine. We can use values in other columns of the same patient to improve the imputation procedure. This is usually better than column imputation which considers only values in one column at a time. We are using expected correlations among different features of the same patient to infer the value of the missing feature. A procedure called k-nearest neighbors imputation fills in a missing cell by looking for patients who are similar to the current patient on the basis of other features, and then uses those known values to impute the missing one. A modern method called multiple imputation, repeatedly invokes imputation to create multiple versions of the data, which can be analyzed to provide an estimate of the variance in the imputed values. Making a decision on whether to remove missing values or to impute: If a variable is mostly measured with only a few missing values, then you should consider imputation If a particular variable has mostly missing values, then you should consider dropping the variable. That variable does not contribute useful information for most patients, and we would have to impute the values for most patients. If the distribution of missing values is in middle-zone between these alternatives, some experts have advised adding indicator variables to mark which values have been imputed, but this recommendation has been disputed by others. From a practical perspective, it may be better to use an analysis method that naturally handles missingness rather than imputing the missing values. Finally, think about how important the feature with missing data is to your question. Could you avoid imputation by answering the question without considering that variable? ## 6. Properties that make answering a research question useful.mp4

### Summary

To determine the value of a scientific inquiry, researchers should evaluate its potential to generate **meaningful real-world impact** rather than just theoretical knowledge. This framework suggests prioritizing questions that address a **significant disease burden** or offer a high probability of **improving clinical outcomes** for a large population. Beyond mere discovery, a truly useful project aims to **enhance human health** by reducing mortality, lowering medical costs, or expanding patient access to services. Ultimately, the most successful research creates **tangible benefits for multiple stakeholders**, ensuring that the resulting data translates into widespread practical improvements.

**Keywords:** Disease burden, Beneficial clinical impact, Human health improvement, Healthcare cost reduction, Improved care access

### Content

We now turn to discussing what makes answering a question useful. There are several factors that are relevant. We present these not as a precise formula for some kind of a utility measure of the question, but as a checklist of aspects of a question that you should consider. Sometimes a small change in the question can make it quite useful. First, consider how many lives are affected. What is the disease burden of the patients who would be affected by your answer? Second, what is the chance that the results will have a beneficial effect on the target community, either the clinicians or the patients. This is important because you want to discover something that is true, but you also want to be able to use that knowledge to improve human health. Finally, what happens as a result of answering the question. Will this lead to a reduction in death, mortality, morbidity, other illnesses? Will it reduce or limit healthcare spending? Will it increase access to care? Does knowing the answer help more than one constituent group amongst patients, providers or professionals and payers of care. The more people who will be helped, the more allies you will have in implementing your answer.

## 10. Types of research questions.mp4

### Summary

This text outlines a structured framework for **categorizing research questions** within clinical data mining, moving from simple summaries to complex underlying mechanisms. The journey begins with **descriptive and exploratory inquiries** that aim to summarize data or discover hidden patterns, followed by **inferential and predictive questions** that seek to establish generalizable relationships and forecast future outcomes. Finally, the framework introduces **causal and deterministic questions**, which move beyond simple associations to identify direct effects and the **fundamental biological machinery** driving specific results. By distinguishing between these levels of inquiry, researchers can better **organize their thinking** and more effectively evaluate the validity of their conclusions.

**Keywords:** Research question types, Clinical data mining, Descriptive questions, Exploratory data analysis, Causal and deterministic

### Content

Our first data mining step is formulating the research question. There are different kinds of research questions and we saw one example. Let's drill down into the types of research questions that can be answered using clinical data. Understanding the kinds of questions is important for you to design your own and to evaluate answers to the questions from others. The types of questions can vary from simple descriptive questions to those that seek a mechanistic explanation. We'll start with the simplest kind of question which is Descriptive. A descriptive question asks for a summary of the data. One example is what proportion of the population has familiar hyper cholesterolmia. Descriptive questions typically involve counts or proportions. The next kind of question is exploratory. An exploratory question attempts to find what patterns might exist in the data set available. Exploration involves some analyses or interpretation of the data. An example is what are the subtypes of autism using the data at your medical center? To do this, we would could try clustering the data using a variety of sets of features to see what groups exist. We would then need to do additional work to ensure that the grouping we find at one institution is also seen at another place. The next kind of question is inferial. An inferial question looks for patterns that go beyond just the particular data set that's available. The goal is to find generalizable knowledge but without necessarily identifying the underlying mechanism. Usually, this involves assessing how changing the average of one measurement or one quantity relates to another. An example, is there a relation between gut bacteria and depression in a collection of patients? Note that this is just looking for a reliable pattern. We don't yet know that the pattern reflects any essential mechanism or cause. The next kind of question is predictive. A predictive question looks for quantitative relationships between some features or characteristics and an outcome or event of interest. The prediction can apply at the level of an individual patient. An example is is which patients are expected to make extensive use of costly medical resources in the coming year. Predictions are based on reliable associations like we saw in the inferial questions but still do not necessarily reflect underlying causes. The next kind of question is causal. Causal question looks for the effect of changes in one variable or one quantity on a second one. A medical question that's causal is for a patient with hypertension and ama whose type 2 diabetes is uncontrolled control with metformin. What is the second best drug? The next and final kind of question is deterministic. Here the question is directly addressing the underlying mechanism. An example of such a question is how does androgen deprivation increase dementia risk. As you can see, there's many kinds of research questions and having such a grouping allows us to organize our thinking.
