## 1. A framework to assess if a data source is useful.mp4

### Summary

To determine if a specific data set is suitable for clinical research, one must evaluate its **organizational structure and documentation** to avoid the burden of fixing unusable information. Researchers must also investigate the **provenance and accessibility** of the data, ensuring that the history of the collection is transparent and that the files are both affordable and legally reachable. Furthermore, it is essential to audit the **data quality and scale**, checking for missing values while confirming the sample size is large enough to capture rare phenomena. Ultimately, this framework serves as a guide to verify that the source contains the **necessary variables or reliable proxies** required to answer a specific scientific question.

**Keywords:** Data model documentation, Data provenance, Accessibility and cost, Data quality issues, Sample size adequacy

### Content

Given that there are numerous choices of data sets to answer a given clinical question, here are some important questions to ask when considering using a particular data source. First, is there a well doumented data model? How are the data organized? If the data set is not well documented or the documentation is wrong, then you'll need to do a significant amount of additional work to make those data usable. Second, where are the data from? This is called data provenence. Is it known? how the data were originally collected and made their way into the data set. Third, are the data accessible and in what form? How do you actually get the data? Some data sets exist but their uses are restricted. You might not be allowed access or excess might be prohibitively expensive. Fourth, what are the known errors in the data? Are some of the data marked as missing? You always need to check data quality and sometimes you need to address quality problems. This can be quite timeconuming. There are additional questions you should consider. For example, does this data set have the data elements corresponding to the patient characteristic that you need to observe? If not, can you use another data element as a proxy for the characteristic you really want to observe? If you're studying rare conditions, is the data set large enough to observe the condition in sufficient numbers? If a condition occurs for one in a million patients, then a data set of only 10,000 patients is not expected to contain any instances of that condition. You need a bigger data set.

## 2. Actors with different interests.mp4

### Summary

The health care system is composed of a diverse array of stakeholders, including patients, providers, and insurers, each driven by **distinct and often conflicting priorities**. While patients prioritize survival and personal recovery, clinicians focus on balancing **clinical efficacy with patient safety**, and regulators seek to ensure **equitable, cost-effective care** for the broader population. These divergent interests create a **natural tension** that fundamentally shapes how medical data is generated, collected, and interpreted. Consequently, researchers must remain mindful of these **inherent biases** when selecting data sources to ensure their findings remain objective and address the needs of multiple interest groups.

**Keywords:** Healthcare system actors, Conflicting stakeholder interests, Clinical data bias, Patient treatment value, Research data privacy

### Content

The primary actors in the health care system are patients and their families, health care professionals and payers as well as the government or regulatory organizations. Patients and consumers ask, "How often can I stay healthy? What's wrong with me? How can I get better?" Clinicians or healthcare providers think, "What is the best practice? How can I maximize benefit while minimizing harm for this patient and for all patients in general?" Payers, government, and regulators are concerned with answering what is best for society, what is cost effective, what is equitable and just. Here's a concrete example. A patient who has a life-threatening illness may want to receive a treatment even if there's a very low chance of success and a high chance of very serious side effects. They will take this chance because it might avert or delay their death. Their doctor may be concerned primarily about the known effects and toxicities of the treatment. And their assessment of the value of the treatment may depend on such factors as the patients age, other medical conditions, and estimates of the quality of life they will get with the treatment. An insurer wants to provide the most benefit for the least cost and so might be reluctant to provide wide access to an expensive treatment which has a very low chance of success. Researchers want to collect detailed data about each patient who was treated in an attempt to provide better care for future patients without violating each individual's right to maintain the privacy of their health information. These different interests lead to a tension in the delivery of health care, in the generation of their data and their use. While this is a course about clinical data and not about health policy, it's important to keep these different interests in mind when defining a research question and choosing a data source to answer the question. Think about who would benefit from answering your question. Think about how their interests could introduce some bias into the data sources that are used, the ones that are produced and collected. Also, look for ways to address more than one audience or interest group at the same time.

## 3. Bias and error from the healthcare system perspective.mp4

### Summary

The healthcare ecosystem generates data that is frequently compromised by **systematic biases and inaccuracies** originating from multiple stakeholders. At the outset, **selection bias** occurs because medical records only capture individuals who choose to seek professional care, often excluding those restricted by financial barriers or personal health literacy. Within the clinical setting, providers may produce **incomplete or delayed documentation** influenced by time constraints or specific financial incentives. Furthermore, the administrative process of **coding and billing** prioritizes reimbursement over clinical detail, often omitting nuances that do not serve a financial purpose. Ultimately, while merging disparate data sources could mitigate these gaps, the process remains fraught with **technical challenges and privacy risks** that can introduce further distortions.

**Keywords:** Healthcare selection bias, Inaccurate medical records, Provider financial incentives, Medical coding errors, Data linkage challenges

### Content

We reviewed the different entities in the healthcare ecosystem. Let's step through different parts of the healthare system again. This time with a focus on ways in which the data produced by each entity might be inaccurate or biased. We'll start with a patient. The patient decides to seek or not seek care. What patient factors might affect their decision? The decision to seek care will be influenced by the patients knowledge of their own health, their knowledge of medicine, and more generally their financial status, and what insurance coverage is providing coverage, if any. This is selection bias. The bias is who is selected to be observed. We're not recording the health status of everyone because we're leaving out those in their normal state of affairs. We're not recording the health status of those who are sick but who treat themselves at home with over-the-counter medications or those who are treated outside of the health system for which we have the records. Some countries have universal health coverage and national medical record systems which help reduce the bias. However, in general, records are neither complete nor is the sample chosen at random. Now we turn to the healthcare provider. They might respond to financial incentives by changing how they decide to treat and which treatment to offer. Those incentives may also affect how they record what they did. The records may be written at the time of service or with some delay afterwards, maybe at night. As a result, these records may be inaccurate or incomplete. For coding and billing, coders assign diagnoses and procedure codes to the medical documentation for the purpose of generating a bill for the services rendered. A medical bill often contains only enough information to construct the bill and get paid, not a complete record of treatment or what happened as a result of the treatment. There may be biases from systematic errors in coding that result from how the clinician documented what they did. Coders may make mistakes themselves or omit codes that are unlikely to be reimbursed. One way to overcome some of these issues is to combine data sources. However, linking records across sources can be technically challenging, can be inaccurate or incomplete and may itself introduce additional biases. Also, there's a need to protect patient privacy and the more you know about a patient and the more you link, the easier it is to identify them.

## 4. Bias and error of exposures and outcomes.mp4

### Summary

Medical research relies on a clear distinction between an **exposure**, which represents an initial event or intervention like a drug treatment, and an **outcome**, which is the subsequent clinical result or cost. By mapping these two elements onto a patient’s timeline, researchers can establish a **logical framework** to measure the relationship between specific causes and their eventual effects. This structured approach is essential because it allows analysts to pinpoint exactly where **biases and data errors** might occur during the observation process. Ultimately, defining these variables serves the critical purpose of ensuring that **quantifying medical rates** and associations remains accurate and reliable.

**Keywords:** Bias and error, Exposure vs outcome, Patient timeline, Research questions, Data collection sources

### Content

Another perspective on biases and errors is to distinguish exposures and outcomes. An exposure is something that could happen to the patient. Examples are diseases, being given some drugs or medical procedures. An outcome is a condition of interest that is assessed as having occurred in the patient timeline at some point after the exposure. Examples are complications of disease or from treatments or other medical outcomes, specific values of laboratory tests, and the cost of care. As we have seen research questions require relating exposures to outcomes or at the minimum quantifying the rate of exposures and the outcomes. This gives us a framework to identify sources of biases and possible errors in the data we collect.

## 5. Claims data.mp4

### Summary

This text explores the nature of **medical claims data**, which are administrative records generated when healthcare providers bill insurers for services rendered. These datasets are uniquely valuable because they **track patient movements across different providers**, capturing essential financial information and diagnostic codes that are often missing from clinical charts. However, the source notes that these records can be inconsistent due to **varying billing incentives** between private and government payers, as well as structural differences in international healthcare systems. Ultimately, while these massive databases are available through entities like **CMS or commercial vendors**, researchers must navigate complex privacy restrictions and potential **coding biases** to use the information effectively.

**Keywords:** Medical claims data, Medical coding process, Healthcare billing records, Health insurance coverage, Clinical data sources

### Content

To collect payment for a medical service, health care professionals send a bill to the health insurer, either a company or the relevant government agency. The bill contains identifying information about the patient, some description of their insurance status, diagnosis and procedure codes, as well as the requested charges. The process of billing is mostly outside the public eye and requires that codes be assigned to each patient to mark the kind of service provided to them. Assigning codes to charts or medical coding is a large business. Note that there can be a difference between the codes that a clinician enters, codes that the hospital bills for, and the codes that the insurance pays for. The bill contains information on the charges for the medical service, which is usually not present in the EMR. These data when collected are referred to as medical claims, billing, or insurance data. Insurance claims and billing records document the costs and payments made for a patient's care. One very useful property of insurance data is that they include everything that insurance paid for. So these records can follow a patient across multiple providers. However, not all billing information is easily combined. In the United States, vision and dental services typically are separate and have different insurance coverage, not connected to the primary healthcare coverage. There can be different ways of assigning codes creating bias. One example arises from the difference between United States and the United Kingdom. In the US, codes are usually assigned at the date of service because the UK has a single provider singlepayer system codes are not needed for billing during individual days of a hospital stay. So the codes can be assigned all on the day of discharge. There are some very large data sets of claims that are available for purchase. These are typically protected by contracts that prohibit the researcher from publicly releasing the data or try to identify who the individual patients are. Some of the data sets include the truven market scan commercial claims and encounters. Another source is optimum clinformatics data marked data are also available from CMS, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, a part of the US government that administers these insurance programs. Medicare provides medical coverage for those 65 years of age and older. Note that Medicare is split into two parts, one covering fee for service, and the other Medicare Advantage providing fixed payments to providers for each year. So providers have rather different incentives under these two Medicare programs, which could lead to systematic differences in what codes are recorded for each patient.

## 6. Common data types in Healthcare.mp4

### Summary

Healthcare information is broadly categorized into **structured data**, which follows a predictable tabular format of rows and columns, and **unstructured data**, which includes complex forms like clinical text, medical imagery, and sensor signals. While structured tables offer organized patient properties, unstructured formats such as **X-rays and electrocardiograms** lack this rigid uniformity and require specialized interpretation. The source emphasizes that these diverse data types are generated at various points throughout a **patient's medical journey**, creating a vital chronological record. Ultimately, understanding this **inherent time ordering** and the specific characteristics of each data format is essential for effectively utilizing information within the healthcare ecosystem.

**Keywords:** Structured healthcare data, Unstructured clinical data, Medical images, Sensor signals, Patient data timelines

### Content

As we have seen all activities in the healthare system produce data. Let us review the important types of healthcare data. First we need to introduce some terminology. Some healthcare data are structured. Structured data have consistent organization. A table with rows and columns. For example, a simple example would be a table that contains information about patients. We could use a column for each property of the patient that we want to represent such as their name or their medical record number, their age, date of birth, and so on. The data for a single patient would occupy one row. Each new patient would get a new row in the table. And note that some data might be missing so that the cell could be filled with a special value to mark this. Usually the code NA is used for this purpose. Some data are unstructured. There are several kinds of unstructured data. Clinical text, images, signals, and so on. Clinical text, for example, although composed out of words, can be quite different from ordinary written language in ways that we will explore in later. For now, Suffice it to say that it's a haiku of acronyms. Some data comes as images which are large two-dimensional arrays of intensity values measuring the degree to which some kind of physical energy is transmitted or absorbed by human tissue. A very common example is a chest X-ray. Sometimes many two-dimensional images are collected together to form representations of volumes. Examples include a computed tomography scan and a magnetic resonance imaging. Some data are signals. These measurements come from a sensor. usually at regularly spaced time intervals. One common example is the electroc cardiogram which records the electrical activity of the heart using electrodes taped to the skin near the heart and hands and feet measured often as several hundred times per second. The key point here is that these textual image and signal data lack the regular organizations seen in our table example. We've seen that healthcare data vary along several dimensions. They occur over different time scales. They're generated at different points in the patient's care journey. and they have different possible values. They can also have different patterns of missing values. We'll talk about the consequences of this in later conversations because the techniques used will depend on the type of data. But for now, remember that there's an inherent time ordering to all the different data types that are generated for a particular patient at various places in the healthcare ecosystem. Knowing this timeline view of the data is essential for effectively using the data.

## 7. Electronic medical record data.mp4

### Summary

Modern medicine has transitioned from physical paper charts to **Electronic Medical Records (EMR)**, which serve as comprehensive digital repositories for every clinical interaction and observation. These systems capture a vast array of information ranging from **standard demographic and diagnostic codes** to advanced biometric data sourced from **genetic testing and wearable consumer devices**. Beyond individual patient care, these massive data aggregates are compiled into **large-scale databases** like MIMIC and Cerner HealthFacts, providing researchers with millions of records for analysis. Ultimately, the text illustrates how **digitized clinical documentation** transforms every provider action and diagnostic result into a structured resource for improving healthcare delivery.

**Keywords:** Electronic medical records, Clinical data types, Patient demographic information, Medical record databases, Clinical care documentation

### Content

We discussed the different types of clinical data, their strengths and weaknesses, as well as the sources of biases and errors. We now turn to the sources of those data sets. The medical record of a patient used to be paper sheets bound together in a folder called a chart. Therefore, we still refer to medical records as charts, but they're now stored in computer systems. These are the electronic medical records or an EMR. Sometimes they're also called the electronic health record or an EHR. These data are collected in the hospital and clinic and contain patient demographic information such as name, their sex and date of birth along with diagnoses and procedure codes. Clinical notes, medication administration records, imaging and lab test results. Newer kinds of EMR data available in some systems come from genetic tests as well as from consumer devices such as wireless blood pressure cuffs and activity monitors. Delivery of clinical care produces documentations about the care. This includes a ress notes written by doctors, nurses and other providers, orders submitted by the doctors, the results of laboratory tests and imaging studies. Practically every click in the EMR software produces some data either in the form of an audit trail or in the form of a new entry into the patient's medical record. An example of widely available electronic medical record data set is the medical information mart for intensive care or mimic which is part of the project physio. net. It contains data on patients in the critical care units of Beth Israel Deaconist Medical Center in Boston, Massachusetts. As of the year 2020, this data set holds records on about 38,000 patients. Another example of medical record data is Cerner Healthfax, which is a commercially available database of medical records from multiple facilities. As of this recording, the data set covers more than 9 years, has more than 1.3 billion lab results, over 84 million patient visits, and more than 151 million drug orders.

## 8. How a patient's exposure might be misclassified.mp4

### Summary

This source illustrates the difficulty of using pharmacy records to accurately track when a patient is actually taking a medication, a problem known as **exposure misclassification**. Because administrative data often fails to capture **informal distributions** like free samples or **patient non-compliance**, such as skipping doses or stopping early, the recorded dates may not align with reality. While researchers attempt to correct these discrepancies by implementing **cleanup rules** to bridge data gaps, these logical adjustments remain **imperfect estimations**. Ultimately, the text highlights the inherent **disconnect between clinical records and human behavior**, reminding us that what is documented is not always what occurred.

**Keywords:** Patient exposure misclassification, Pharmacy claims data, Medication adherence, Data cleanup rules, Prescription drug records

### Content

Let us think about how a patient's exposure might be mclassified. We'll use exposure to medication as an illustration, but the ideas apply to other kinds of exposures as well. Suppose a doctor writes a prescription. The prescription is for a 30-day supply of the medication with one refill. The doctor knows that there'll be a short delay in sending the prescription to the pharmacy and having the pharmacy fill the prescription because the patient cannot start taking the medication immediately. The doctor gives the patient a 15-day free sample to cover this gap. The patient starts taking the free example and gets the prescription filled at their local pharmacy. Suppose that happens say 7 days after the doctor visit. The patient starts the medication from the pharmacy on day 16 after the free sample runs out and continues taking the medication. Shortly before it runs out, the patient orders a refill. Picks that up from the pharmacy and takes the medication until the refill runs out. Let's say the sequence of events is what actually happened. What would we see in the pharmacy claims data? There would be two 30-day orders filled. The first would start at day seven during the 15-day period when the patient was using the free sample. However, the pharmacy claims data would not contain any record of the use of the 15-day free sample. There would be a 7-day gap during which the patient was taking the medication but is not recorded in the claims data. Suppose the patient occasionally forgets to take the medication. So, the first 30-day supply then lasts longer than 30 days. This would delay when they request a refill. So, the pharmacy claims data would show another gap. Suppose the patient orders the refill but stops taking that supply after a week because they feel completely better. Now, the claims data show them taking a medication for longer than they actually did. Therefore, the time the patient is actually exposed to the drug is different from what can be inferred by classifying exposures on the basis of claims data. We can construct cleanup rules that try to produce more accurate estimates by filling in the gaps. A rule might, for example, be that fill in the small gaps such as less than 7 days in the record. However, these rules They're not perfect.

## 9. How a patient's outcome could be misclassified.mp4

### Summary

Medical records often contain inaccuracies because diagnostic codes may be used for insurance justification before a patient is officially diagnosed. To address this, researchers employ **electronic phenotyping**, a strategy that uses multiple data points—such as repeated diagnoses, specific medication logs, or procedure codes—to verify a patient's true clinical status. These verification methods can be validated through **labor-intensive manual chart reviews** or simulated to determine how much error an analysis can tolerate. Ultimately, the text emphasizes that **relying on diverse data sources** and clinical expertise is essential to minimize the risk of misclassifying patient outcomes in health research.

**Keywords:** Patient outcome misclassification, Electronic phenotyping, Diagnosis code accuracy, Medical record validation, Clinical data sources

### Content

Now let's think about how a patient's outcome could be mclassified. A common situation is the assignment of codes when the clinical status of a patient is not yet certain. If a patient is suspected to have diabetes, the code for diabetes might need to be used in their medical record in order to justify ordering certain diagnostic tests such as the glucose tolerance test. If it turns out that the patient does not have the diabetes condition, the earlier codes might still remain in their record. What can we do to uce such mclassification biases. As a simple fix, we can require multiple mentions of a diagnosis code for us to believe that the patient indeed has the condition. We can require that along with a diagnostic code for the condition, there also must be an occurrence of a disease-specific procedure. The procedure code would only be used if the procedure had actually been done on the patient. This is a higher bar than just requiring the diagnosis code because it's very unlikely that a procedure would be done unless the patient has the condition. Similar A medication that is very specific for a disease might be required to be present in the record to conclude that the patient had the disease. These strategies or huristics to determine if the outcome indeed occurred in the patient's timeline go by various names. We will refer to them as electronic phenotyping in our conversation. We can validate the error rate or mclassification rate of these strategies for electronic phenotyping by comparing to manual review of the patient's chart. Their prim medical record. This is the most widely accepted way to assess, but it is labor intensive as it requires a clinician to carefully read each chart. It also requires approval of the local institutional review board. Alternatively, we can put bounds on the net effect of mclassification by constructing a computer simulation that adds random mclassification to the exposure or the outcome measurement to see the effect on the answer to the question at hand. Some analyses might be robust to a very wide range of mclass. ification rates whereas others might not be. So in general when using clinical data use multiple sources because one source may protect from a weakness in another source or may help in at least estimating the error rate in the other source. For example, if metformin was used to treat diabetes and nothing else, then finding patients who were on metformin but do not have the diagnosis code for diabetes allows us to quantify the rate at which the diabetes code misses. true diabetic patients. Of course, in real life, such simple correspondences are rare and clinical expertise is needed in formulating as well as evaluating the strategies for assertaining the exposure and the outcomes we care about.

Current taxonomy is frequently misleading and fails to describe the complexity of the entirety of the US health care system. **Health **is a misnomer, because most activity involves illness. **Health care **and **medical care **are not synonymous. **Prevention **requires tools that are often unfamiliar because educational, behavioral, and social inter-ventions, not usually considered to be part of medicine, may be most effective for many diseases. **Provider **does not accurately describe the dozens of different professions and organizations required for a pa-tient’s care. **Payers **are paid not to pay too easily; **insurers **do only mod-est amounts of insuring because government and employers accept most risk. Economic concepts of **cost **and **value **are ambiguous, as mea-surement is elusive and because one segment’s cost is another’s value. **Market **is a misnomer because few prices are transparent and many are controlled. Above all, US health care is not a **system, **as it is nei-ther coordinated by a central entity nor governed by individuals and institutions that interact in predictable ways. Since 1900, US life span (at birth) has lengthened by 30 years, or 62%, and today it is estimated at 81 years for women and 76 years for men.1 From the 1950s through the 1980s, gains in longevity were attributable to a combination of increasingly sophisticated, scientifi-cally based medical care as well as by effective public health and health Health care in the United States includes a vast array of complex interrelationships among those who receive, provide, and finance care. In this article, publicly available data were used to identify trends in health care, principally from 1980 to 2011, in the source and use of funds (“economic anatomy”), the people receiving and organizations providing care, and the resulting value created and health outcomes. In 2011, US health care employed 15.7% of the workforce, with expenditures of $2.7 trillion, doubling since 1980 as a percentage of US gross domestic product (GDP) to 17.9%. Yearly growth has decreased since 1970, especially since 2002, but, at 3% per year, exceeds any other industry and GDP overall. Government funding increased from 31.1% in 1980 to 42.3% in 2011. Despite the increases in resources devoted to health care, multiple health metrics, including life expectancy at birth and survival with many diseases, shows the United States trailing peer nations. The findings from this analysis contradict several common assumptions. Since 2000, (1) price (especially of hospital charges [+4.2%/y], professional services [3.6%/y], drugs and devices [+4.0%/y], and administrative costs [+5.6%/y]), not demand for services or aging of the population, produced 91% of cost increases; (2) personal out-of-pocket spending on insurance premiums and co-payments have declined from 23% to 11%; and (3) chronic illnesses account for 84% of costs overall among the entire population, not only of the elderly. Three factors have produced the most change: (1) consolidation, with fewer general hospitals and more single-specialty hospitals and physician groups, producing financial concentration in health systems, insurers, pharmacies, and benefit managers; (2) information technology, in which investment has occurred but value is elusive; and (3) the patient as consumer, whereby influence is sought outside traditional channels, using social media, informal networks, new public sources of information, and self-management software. These forces create tension among patient aims for choice, personal care, and attention; physician aims for professionalism and autonomy; and public and private payer aims for aggregate economic value across large populations. Measurements of cost and outcome (applied to groups) are supplanting individuals’ preferences. Clinicians increasingly are expected to substitute social and economic goals for the needs of a single patient. These contradictory forces are difficult to reconcile, creating risk of growing instability and political tensions. Economic Anatomy In 2011, spending on health was $2.7 trillion (Figure 2) or 17.9% of gross domestic product (GDP). The health care sectors employed more than 21 million people, accounting for 15.7% of the US work-force (Figure 3). In 2011, there were approximately 800 000 phy-sicians and 2.7 million nurses in the United States. Since 1980, the rate of increase of different categories of health care spending has varied from year to year. Administrative costs have more than doubled (from 3% [1980] to 7% [2010] of total spend-ing with growth at 5.6%/y); but since 2000, most (84%) of the in-crease is attributable to growth of drugs and devices (+4.0%/y), pro-fessional services (3.6%/y), and hospital care (4.2%/y) (Figure 2). However, since 1970, the **rate **of average yearly increase of total ex-penditures has declined (with some fluctuations), a trend espe-cially marked since 2002 (Figure 4). These data contradict a commonly held belief that aging of the population and increased demand for services have driven spending historically. Between 2000 and 2011, increase in price (par-ticularly of drugs, medical devices, and hospital care), not intensity of service or demographic change, produced most of the increase in health’s share of GDP. We have attempted no analysis of the individual effects of intro-duction of the Medicare Part D drug benefit, behavior in anticipation of the 2010 Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (PPACA), the 2007 recession, actions to reduce utilization by insurers, or efficiency efforts by clinicians and health care organizations. Some of those fac-tors ameliorated while others exacerbated the rate of growth. Since the 1990s, US polls have consistently shown high levels of patient satisfaction with ex-periences with individual physicians and nurses, whereas insurers, hospitals, and Medicare fare less well on satisfaction ratings.29 Simi-lar high satisfaction levels are also found within the OECD despite very different health system philosophies, organization, and insur-ance programs.30 High levels of US satisfaction with personal as-pects of care confound health economists and policy advocates who are most mindful of costs and see shortfalls in quality, and also in-fluence political debate over Medicare and Medicaid. The trend has accelerated since passage of the 2010 PPACA.31 Mortality in the United States (all causes) from common con-ditions compares unfavorably with mortality in other OECD coun-tries, and the relative trend in changes in mortality rate is also un-favorable (eFigure 8 in the Supplement). While longevity (at birth) is increasing in all countries, the US rate of improvement began to diverge beginning in the 1980s for men and 1990s for women. Sig-nificant differences in morbidity are also found between the United States and the OECD in total burden of disease (as adjusted for age, income, and other demographic factors), with only cancer (all types) reaching equivalence for all 6 decades between 1960 and 2008 (eFigure 9 and eTable 2 in the Supplement). Possible causes of this departure from international norms were highlighted in a 2013 Institute of Medicine report32 and have been ascribed to many factors, only some of which are attributed to medi-cal care financing or delivery. These include differences in cultural norms that affect healthy behaviors (gun ownership, unprotected sex, drug use, seat belts), obesity, and risk of trauma. Others are di-rectly or indirectly attributable to differences in care, such as de-lays in treatment due to lack of insurance and fragmentation of care between different physicians and hospitals.32,33 Some have also sug-gested that unfavorable US performance is explained by higher risk of iatrogenic disease, drug toxicity, hospital-acquired infection, and a cultural preference to “do more,” with a bias toward new technol-ogy, for which risks are understated and benefits are unknown.34 However, the breadth and consistency of the US underperfor-mance across disease categories suggests that the United States pays a penalty for its extreme fragmentation, financial incentives that fa-vor procedures over comprehensive longitudinal care, and ab-sence of organizational strategy at the individual system level. Consolidation and Industrialization Consolidation is the rule in most industries once they reach a cer-tain size and maturity. Consolidation can be triggered by changes in regulatory policy, a new entrant that spurs defensive action by in-cumbents, a search for competitive advantage through lower cost (known as scale advantage), or to achieve greater market share. Since the 1980s, the industries of banking, energy, airlines, railroads, au-tomobiles, and media have been altered by cycles of mergers. These provide lessons for health care, and they highlight potential pit-falls, especially in anticompetitive behavior, increasing prices, and exploitation of and less choice for consumers. A recurring lesson is that once begun, consolidation can proceed astonishingly quickly. The United States has seen rapid consolidation over the past de-cade among insurers, physician practices, and pharmacies (Figure 10 and eFigure 6 in the Supplement). Information and Information Technology To foster communication across increasingly large and complex in-dustrial enterprises, health care firms have made substantial invest-ments in IT. Since information plays a central role in every aspect of health care, it has been expected to be pivotal in improving the ef-fectiveness of the system.47 Moreover, investment in IT can gener-ally be made without alienating either political progressives or con-servatives, and its payoff is sufficiently long-term that there is little immediate accountability. Accordingly, federal appropriations and private investment in IT have been increasing steadily over the last 10 years (Figure 12), with spending on IT reaching $33 billion in 2011, or 3% of total hospital and facility expenditures. Has this increased spending been valuable? There have been 2 main goals: (1) increasing the efficiency of the administration and management of the system and (2) improv-ing the quality of clinical care (largely by integrating the flow of in-formation required to support coordination across multiple provid-ers and over time). From the high (and increasing) level of accounting, insurance, and management cost alone, it appears that IT has had little effect on the first goal. Figure 13 captures one credible esti-mate of the current cost of billing, insurance, and related costs. Ad-ministrative costs related to the provision of services is 13% for phy-sicians and 8.5% for hospitals, whereas that for insurers is 12.3% for private payers and 3.5% for public programs such as Medicare and Medicaid. These costs compare unfavorably with what virtually ev-ery other care system in the world spends on accounting, insur-ance, and management costs. Health care also compares poorly with other service industries in its transactions efficiency and cost, as il-lustrated by the workforce and personnel required to process $1 bil-lion in revenue (Figure 13B). For instance, US billing and insurance costs are 13.0% of revenue vs 6.6% in Canada.50,51 Other countries that also have combinations of public and private insurers (eg, Ger-many, France, Japan) have lower rates than the United States. In-formation technology investments may have reduced costs from what they would otherwise have been, and likely only partially ac-count for their enormity, but have not made US system administra-tion efficient by any measure. More promising but unproven has been the investment in IT to support better-coordinated clinical care. The complexity of what jama.com JAMA November 13, 2013 Volume 310, Number 18 1957 needed to be coordinated produced multiple preconditions for suc-cess, each difficult to achieve. First, all relevant information had to be captured digitally. Second, information formats had to be suffi-ciently standardized so that the captured information could be de-ployed in the multiple IT systems that might use it. Third, the indi-viduals and institutions involved needed to agree to share the information (with appropriate protection for privacy and security in-terests). Fourth, application programs needed to be developed to convert the data into useful information for clinicians. The complex-ity of accomplishing these tasks in the million-facility US system is truly extraordinary, and for many years it largely frustrated the ef-forts of IT missionaries to create value through care coordination. The last decade has nevertheless seen some substantial gains. This is true of both individual care systems (the VA and Kaiser Perma-nente are among the more commented-on success stories), and re-gional systems, which typically invest 4% to 5% of revenue in IT, twice the rate of smaller systems.52 In contrast to the situation in 2000, 95% of hospitals have now adopted electronic medical records, al-though there is great variety in their sophistication (eFigure 13 in the Supplement).  Patients’ expectation for easy access to clinicians has spawned “concierge” and alternative practice arrangements in which indi-viduals, rather than being part of large patient “panels,” are mem-bers of a small practice. The popularity of these concierge practices reflects the preferences of both physicians and consumers. Con-sumers, who increasingly extend beyond the wealthy and include those with chronic or less common diseases,63 purchase assured ac-cess and additional time in their search for expert advice, not solely procedures or interventions. As more physicians opt out of Medicare64 and private insurance, patients who choose to pur-chase preferred access become a visible reminder of the shortcom-ings of large hospitals or group practices and their reimbursement contracts. In response, some states (eg, Massachusetts, Oregon) have considered discouraging growth of concierge medicine via regu-lation and physician licensing laws. In this regard, the United States is recapitulating the lessons learned in the United Kingdom, Canada, and Europe, where (unsuccessful) attempts to limit patient choice and their ability to pay directly for care occurred in the early years of country-wide reorganization of health care financing. Today, the futility of such restrictions is reflected by the inability of state-controlled health systems to restrict access to procedures that en-hance quality of life (eg, major joint replacement, cataract surgery) but that do not affect mortality or for which benefit is subjective. Consumers are also changing the conduct of clinical research by organizing their own studies, enhancing trial recruitment, and ensur-ing flow of patients to specialized investigators and centers.65 Among the first examples of this trend were patients with human immunode-ficiency virus infection and those affected by poorly understood or rare diseases, such as autism, inherited myopathies, and amyotrophic lat-eral sclerosis; however, this is now extending to many common con-ditions, including dementia and cancer. These expressions of patients’ desiresareoccurringlargelyoutsideofestablishedinstitutions,andthey have the potential to challenge all of them. Implications Based on this review of the anatomy of health care in 2013, the US “system” has performed relatively poorly, by some measures, de-spite the extraordinary economic success of many of its partici-pants. Outcomes have improved, but more slowly than in the past and more slowly than in comparable countries. Costs have tripled in real terms over the past 2 decades. In the last 8 years, the trend in cost has moderated, and each of the 3 factors discussed herein (consolidation of insurers and health systems, health information, and the patient as consumer) most likely played a role, especially the development of more systematic management of what remains a highly fragmented system. A general drive to measure and manage for value and accountability, for outcomes, and for spending has emerged, and it appears to have sustained momentum. In contrast to that good news, it is also clear that disruptive tensions are sur-facing. Their importance is just now being recognized. Health care is caught in an iron triangle of conflicting expecta-tions among patients, clinicians, and public health and govern-ment policy makers (Figure 14). Not all of the forces can be satis-fied and some are mutually exclusive. The criteria insurers use are not transparent to the patient, be they based on clinical outcomes, discounts to the insurer, or strictly commercial factors (such as exclusivity or other favorable contract provisions). Physicians’ historical autonomy and professionalism are at odds with the corporatization of “Big Med,” “Big Pay,” “Big Data,” and con-solidation of the industry. Above all, the patient’s desire for the cli-nician’s best advice and expert attention may be in tension with fi-nancial incentives to restrict diagnostic tests and procedures. Today, incentives for quality and tools for its measurement are rudimen-tary. Unless value to the patient is judged by much more sophisti-cated nonfinancial measures (objectively by outcomes and subjec-tively by perception of the care process, adaptation to disability, and emotional accommodation to illness), physicians and patients will be driven apart when they should be allies.66 Moreover, public health and social policy goals are applied to groups of people, not individuals. Groups balance cost, morbidity, and mortality as measured by averages and trends, many of which we have herein reviewed. This reflects a shift in attention from the individual to the group, raising risks that norms for panels of pa-tients will supplant those that affect the individual patient. It is not clear that patients will be willing, or even that they should be asked, to shift their allegiance and trust to a health system or insurer rather than a clinician. Part of what has characterized the United States for decades—in some ways one of its strengths—is the focus on the in-dividual. However, as the US health care system is realigned, the larger focus on population health may be inevitable. This change in optimizing care and cost for a population rather than an individual will be disruptive, but its effects will not be known for decades. The path the nation seem to be embarking on, toward further integration of clinical services and consolidation of payers and health systems, has been pursued without an overall strategy. No single en-tity, not even the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, can com-mand the pieces of a system so complex or having so many dispa-rate groups, each with different expectations. The biggest driver of change is organizational consolidation and integration, which has generally been pursued under logics of individual-institution com-petitive success in geographies or business sectors rather than as a part of a national strategy. Although some elements may be favor-ably aligned to improving US health outcomes and reducing costs (such as IT and rewards for high-value scientific innovations), oth-ers are not. An important concern is that patient engagement may be compromised under the evolution. While a national shared strategy for the system is not a realistic option, a national conversation based on the data can provide help-ful structure that can support continued evolution toward financing and delivery of care that provides better value. In particular, a stron-ger collective grasp on the economic structure of care delivery is needed. For example, Christensen et al60 have articulated 3 quite dif-ferent functions (each with distinct business models) that are re-quired to provide health care: one that solves complicated diagnos-tic problems, a second that adds value through an intervention or procedure, and a third that manages relationships and information. These differences are exemplified by neurology (solves complicated diagnostic problems), hip replacement or cardiac surgery (reproduc-ible intervention), and effective care of chronic diseases (manages re-lationships and information flow). Each requires different payment schemes and each has distinctly different organizational needs for them to be effective; yet most payers and health care provider enti-ties ignore the challenge to optimally manage each component. The characterization of the current state of US health care reported in this article leads to the conclusion that institutional structure reflects nei-ther the underlying economics nor the operating requirements of pro-viding maximum value at minimum cost. Other inferences are warranted from the information we have pre-sented. The health care system has not adequately incorporated the evolving understanding of social, personal, and cultural dynamics into the design of health services and information flow. For instance, evi-dence has accumulated since the 1970s that successful, sustained weight loss, medication adherence, and suicide prevention occur only when the approach is tailored to those with particular educational and social backgrounds, plus peer support, yet these lessons are rarely re-flected in existing programs. As another example, the OECD countries have done much better than the United States in serving populations and conserving resources, but lessons that are known appear to be largely ignored, and which factors account for the difference are not fully understood. Similarly, there is limited understanding of the rea-sons behind variation in cost and outcome between US regions and counties, even those that persist after correction for different demo-graphics, education, disease burden, and income levels. At the highest level, the US health system is struggling to adapt to the triad, the iron triangle, of goals, desires, and expectations. The conflict among patient desires, physician interests, and social policy is certain to increase. Those tensions will likely become a palpable force that may hinder care integration and inhibit other changes that favor improved outcome and savings. The usual approach is to ad-dress each constituency in isolation rather than optimizing efforts across them. The triangle will need to be reconciled. This is the chief challenge of the next decade. Given the changes described in this article, what develop-ments might stop forward momentum? The first and most salient is resistance to change by patients and the citizenry at large. Such resistance would almost certainly be expressed politically, and with great stridency. This risk could be triggered by a new kind of medi-cal paternalism, one that substitutes “Big Med,” Washington, or “my insurer knows best” for “the doctor knows best.” The medical pro-fession has spent decades discouraging medical paternalism in all its manifestations. To have it reemerge in another form would be un-fortunate and counterproductive. Patients would receive medical paternalism very poorly, as may be reflected by growing public op-position to President Obama’s health care agenda and the PPACA.31 Another destabilizing risk is failure to invest adequately in care improvement. Service innovation is a poor stepchild, with declin-ing current levels of investment and very low historical invest-ment. It will be important to understand what and where service in-novations work and to invest in devising new approaches to refractory clinical process problems, especially those for chronic dis-ease and those for which behavioral and social interventions are re-quired. To that end, insurers should be required to disclose anony-mized information on patterns of care that only they posses in claims databases, rather than treat them as proprietary. A corollary risk is to curtail investment in new technology and its supporting science. However, those in companies and in Congress who must make investment decisions are discouraged by the long gesta-tion period of advances that could fundamentally change diseases for jama.com JAMA November 13, 2013 Volume 310, Number 18 1961 which neither effective preventive measures nor treatments exist. We have suggested previously67 that new financial vehicles need to be cre-ated that can complement existing sources of public and private funds, especially those to support high-risk/high-reward directions. However, inthecurrentclimateofausterity, inwhichthecostofnewmedicaltech-nology often seems more obvious than its clinical value, the risk of lim-iting or curtailing funding for research is very real, although short-sighted. It is equally clear that the productivity of the research enter-prise needs to be radically improved. In addition, risk is appreciable that the current patchwork “sys-tem,” even with the changes of recent legislation and progress in the private sector, will simply collapse under its own weight, especially as the population ages. Some have even advocated this outcome as a necessary step toward a national single-payer system or a way to lower Medicare costs.68 Given the tensions implicit in health care, such a failure would be chaotic and its outcome uncontrollable. Could current tensions presage favorable outcomes? Physi-cians and advance practice nurses, spurred by a new, younger gen-eration, might prove highly receptive to altered incentives, bring new objectivity, and embrace broader measures of success, such as those that reflect the value of their clinical judgment and their ability to engage patients in decisions having major gravity. Physicians and nurses, not “Big Med,” “Big Pay,” or the government, could become the main sources of service innovation. Similarly, altogether new en-trants may enter the arena and would fully mobilize people, infor-mation, and technology in ways not currently envisioned. Disruptive change could also come from patients, as informa-tion asymmetries are lessened and as patients show increasing awareness of the emotional costs of high-intensity interventions that have little realistic likelihood of prolonging life or improving its qual-ity. These are exemplified by examination of routine screening prac-tices in primary care medicine, as well as new evidence from pallia-tive care and oncology that permit early identification of diseases at a stage when intensive treatment would likely be futile. In such examples, patient desires and objective outcomes are aligned to a greater degree than many have suspected.69 Are these realistic outcomes or just pipe dreams? Each pro-posal implies a choice. Who is to guide such choices? A new discus-sion—ideally out of the political arena and with self-interest held at bay—among all of the involved constituencies can do so. What can guide better choices than have been made in the past? Perhaps a conversation, fully informed by the facts and acknowledging per-spectives among those who receive, provide, and finance health care, can do better than the political acrimony of the past few years.
