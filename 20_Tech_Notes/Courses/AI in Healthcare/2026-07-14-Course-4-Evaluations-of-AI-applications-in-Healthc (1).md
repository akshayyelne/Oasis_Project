## 1. AI Output.mp4

### Summary

While high accuracy is the traditional benchmark for healthcare AI, this text argues that true evaluation must shift focus from model development to the **outcome-action pairing**. Evaluating a tool requires looking past the prediction itself to consider the **window of action**, which determines whether a clinical team has enough lead time to effectively intervene. Most researchers mistakenly prioritize data architecture, yet the source emphasizes that the utility of a model depends entirely on **how the output is used** to change a patient's treatment pathway. By centering the **action side of the equation**, developers can ensure that AI tools are not just statistically impressive but are successfully **integrated into clinical care** to improve real-world medical outcomes.

**Keywords:** AI healthcare evaluation, Prediction lead time, Outcome action pairing, Model integration, Window of action

### Content

So, we want to know how to evaluate an AI solution in healthcare. Let's say that you have a prediction model for 30-day hospital readmissions and it has 94% accuracy. So, what's next? Many people stop or get stuck here when they are evaluating an AI solution, but for us, this is where we're going to start. So, let's say that you have longitudinal data for a patient. We can think of a patient's timeline with different events. You have a window of obser ation where you're collecting and aggregating data. This observation window could be the impatient hospital stay for a readmissions example. Then you have a set time point where you start predicting an event. In our example, this could be a time of hospital discharge. You make a prediction. You have a number. Let's say the probability of something happening is 0.92. And that event can happen within the next hour or the next n years depending on what you're predicting. If you are predicting patient deterioration, it may happen within minutes or hours after the observation period. However, if you are predicting Alzheimer's disease based on genotyping and family history, your event might not occur for many years, maybe even decades. So, this gives you your window of action. And depending on the prediction, this action window can be categorized as acute or long-term. So, one must think about whether the action to my output has to happen immediately or is it something that's going to happen over the next year, 5 years, or 10 years? Studies have shown that regardless of acute or long-term actions, early warning lead times give more opportunities for action. So, the further in advance I can make my prediction, the more time one would have to respond with an action to that output. If I'm predicting patient deterioration and my prediction occurs 2 minutes before or a cardiac arrest that gives the clinical team very little time to mitigate that event. However, if my prediction occurs 2 hours before a cardiac arrest, then that gives the team time to mitigate that problem and respond to the output. This is a core construct to understand. Now, most time and effort goes into thinking about the data collection and model development. There are thousands of papers, books, and meetings focused solely on on the development of an AI model, the clinical data to be used, the model architecture needed, etc. Interestingly, almost no effort goes into the action side of the equation. You make a prediction, you publish the paper, and then you're done. Well, here's where I want you to broaden your thinking about AI evaluation. What we need to think about is how do we move beyond predictions and start thinking about the action side of the evaluation? How will the prediction be used? used and by whom so that we can build better models and make sure they get integrated into care. This is why I want you to start thinking beyond predictions. And to do this, we will start with a framework that allows us to conceptualize outcome action pairing. You may have previously heard about outcome action pairing or OAP. In OAP, the outcome is the purpose of the AI model, disease diagnosis, risk stratification, or event prediction. The action is the step that can be taken based on the outcome that will improve in some way medical care. A different treatment pathway based on risk, a new treatment based on a diagnosis or scheduled follow-up which can prevent a hospital readmission. When we think about an AI solution, we must understand the outcome action pairing.

## 2. Clinical Evaluation and Uptake.mp4

### Summary

To achieve widespread adoption in healthcare, AI systems must navigate complex **legal and privacy frameworks** while prioritizing **explainability** to earn the trust of medical professionals. The core of this process is a rigorous **three-part clinical evaluation** designed to ensure that a tool is safe, effective, and scientifically sound. This begins with establishing a **valid clinical association** to prove the AI's logic aligns with real-world medical evidence, followed by **analytical validation** to confirm the model is technically accurate and reproducible. Finally, the process culminates in **clinical validation**, which measures whether the technology actually produces **meaningful health outcomes** for patients. Together, these pillars form a essential regulatory structure that transforms experimental algorithms into reliable instruments for modern medicine.

**Keywords:** Medical liability, AI explainability, Valid clinical association, Analytical validation, Clinical validation

### Content

The uptake of AI solutions by health care systems will depend upon relevant laws and regulations on medical liability which vary widely across the United States and around the world. There are challenges regarding liability, data security, and patient privacy. It is important to think about the harm that may be caused by an AI solution. Also, crucial to clinical uptake is a good level of explanability. That is, the AI solution provides information that helps explain how it came to decision, thus increasing the level of trust for doctors. While the blackbox model can be integrated in the clinical setting, there's more hesitation from the medical community. This is also a topic that will be covered in more detail in other lectures. Moving on, another component necessary to understand utility includes the clinical evaluation of the AI solution. The International Medical Device Regulator Forum or the IMDRF has developed a framework for clinical evaluation that was adopted globally including by the US Food and Drug Administration or the FDA. The framework is used to assess the risk and impact of AI solutions and to demonstrate assurance of the safety, effectiveness and performance of the AI solution. Clinical evaluation has three parts and we'll go through each of these. The first part is valid clinical association. A valid clinical association refers to the extent to which the model output is clinically accepted or wellfounded based on established scientific framework or body of evidence and corresponds accurately in the real world to the healthcare situation and conditions identified by the AI solution. This is the scientific validity of the question or purpose of the AI solution. Consider prior work that found pneumonia patients with asthma were less likely to die than pneumonia patients without asthma. The AI solution predicted that death in pneumonia patients found asthma as a protective feature. However, this was not supported by scientific evidence and it was later determined that patients with pneumonia and asthma received escalated care. Once this was accounted for in the model, the protective effect of asthma on pneumonia death disappeared. It is important to have a valid clinical association between your output and features if you expect clinical acceptance or clinical uptake. The second aspect of clinical eval ation is analytical validation. Does the model correctly process the input data to generate reliable, accurate and precise output data? This type of evaluation requires an understanding of the clinical data used to develop the model and the transparency in the reporting of the processing of training data as well as a clear understanding of the labeled input and output variables. For example, one would need to know if patients are included and or excluded systematically. and is there a valid reason for the exclusion criteria? How are output data labeled and is there subjectivity in the labeling of the data? What are the data prep-processing procedures and can the training data be regenerated from the source population? In other words, analytical validation provides objective evidence that the model was correctly constructed. This refers to the accuracy, repeatability, and reproducibility of the model. These are the common criteria used to evaluate AI solutions and you should be familiar with these concepts from other courses. Finally, the third step of clinical evaluation is clinical validation. Clinical validation measures the ability of an AI solution to generate a clinically meaningful output in the target disease, situation, or condition intended. Clinically meaningful refers to the impact the AI solution may have on the health of an individual or a population. For example, if an AI solution that predicts surgical sight infection is deployed in the hospital, it should reduce the number of surgical sight infections or decrease the lead time to diagnosis of a surgical sight infection, which in the end could improve patient outcomes. As another example, it would be important to validate that a model developed to predict care need was indeed predicting the patients need for medical services rather than their ability to access medical services. Clinical validation is necessary for any AI solution. Clinical evaluation is a key component of the regulatory environment and these three criteria should be part of any AI evaluation.

## 3. Clinical Utility of AI Solution.mp4

### Summary

The core purpose of this text is to define **clinical utility** as the fundamental metric for determining if an artificial intelligence tool is actually worth implementing in a healthcare setting. The author emphasizes that a model's value is not found in its raw data, but in the **output-action pairing**, which requires a clear link between a prediction and a tangible medical intervention. By identifying a specific **beneficiary** and asking critical questions about the **feasibility and cost of acting** on a result, developers can ensure their technology solves a meaningful problem rather than existing in a vacuum. Ultimately, the source argues that for any AI solution to be successful, there must be a **practical ability to mitigate or address** the specific outcome predicted by the algorithm.

**Keywords:** Clinical utility, Healthcare AI evaluation, Output action pairing, Model outcome impact, Decision mitigation strategies

### Content

So let's start our discussion of AI evaluations. We will start with clinical utility. Clinical utility of an AI solution relates to its applicability and impact on the healthcare system. It requires identifying the beneficiary of the AI solution and understanding what action can be taken based on the model outcome that will improve something for the beneficiary. These two components action and outcome will allow you to better understand the problem addressed by the AI solution. and if the problem is worth solving with AI. When we start thinking about evaluating an AI solution, we need to consider what action can be taken based on the model output. Does a mitigating action or therapy exist? What is the ability to intervene or who needs to take the action? What is the lead time offered by the prediction? What are the logistics and costs of the intervention? And what are the incentives for acting on the output. When we start evaluating an AI solution, these are the types of questions we need to be asking. And if these questions are asked upfront, you can optimize your model and ensure clinical uptake. I want you to start thinking about these questions and how they will be used to evaluate an AI solution in healthcare. Start with a problem you are trying to solve with AI and then ask, is that problem worth solving? To help you answer this question for any AI solution, we will start by analyzing an action that can be paired with a model outcome, something we refer to as the output action pairing or what the cool people will call the OAP. So the first rule in evaluating an AI solution is that for every good AI solution or prediction, there should be the ability to act upon or mitigate the output. This is the principal component of utility assessment.

## 5. Decision Curves.mp4

### Summary

Decision curve analysis serves as a vital tool for evaluating the **clinical utility** of a predictive model by measuring its **net benefit** across various threshold probabilities. While traditional metrics like the **area under the ROC curve** focus strictly on accuracy, they often fail to identify the model that provides the most practical value in real-world scenarios. A model with a lower overall accuracy can actually be superior if its predictions cross into a region of **higher utility**, where the benefits of taking an action outweigh the associated costs. By calculating these outcomes against a **default indifference line**, researchers can determine which specific decision-making threshold maximizes gains rather than just minimizing errors. Ultimately, this approach ensures that the chosen AI model translates into **meaningful clinical impact** rather than just statistical precision.

**Keywords:** Decision curve analysis, Net benefit, Clinical utility, ROC curve limitations, Threshold probability

### Content

A decision curve can be used to derive the net benefit of a model across different threshold probabilities. For simplicity, consider two models which show ROC curves for two readmission prediction models with the yellow curve having the larger area under the ROC curve, let's say above 0.8, compared to the blue curve which has an area under the ROC curve of around 0.7. Given estimated costs and benefits of the actions possible to ate a readmission, be it schedule of an appointment with a primary care provider or extended prophylaxis treatment during the impatient stay. There is a region of higher utility than what the best model allows to be achieved. The blue model actually has a smaller area under the curve compared to the yellow model, but the blue model has a higher utility based on the net benefit of the readmission preventing actions based on the model's predictions. We see this because The blue ROC curve crosses over into the region of higher net utility on the graph. In fact, several other models could be created during the process of learning that have even higher utility, but usually those will never be considered because the choice of the best model is often based on measures such as area under the curve rather than utility. This example illustrates how a two-step process to evaluate net benefit will fail to uncover that a model more useful than the best model model based on area under the ROC curve exists. Again, because the blue ROC curve extends into the region of higher benefit, this model has higher utility than the model represented by the yellow ROC curve, even though it has lower area under the ROC curve. To help you understand how utility is calculated, here is a simple example. Let's say I have built a gearbox and the total cost to manufacture the gearbox is $300. If the gearbox is good, I can sell it and make $20. If it is bad, I lose the cost of the device and the entire system, which is $300. So now, let's say that I build a classifier and it tells me if the gearbox is good or bad, and it's 95% accurate. So, if my gear box is good and the classifier says it's good, I get the $20. If it's bad and the classifier says it's bad, I lose $50 because I don't send it out on the market. However, if the gear box is good, but the classifier says it's bad, then I also lose $50. But if the gearbox is bad, and my classifier says it's good, I send it out on the market and I lose the total $300. A priority, you know that 5% of the gear boxes are bad. So, we can set that as your fixed value default. Using these numbers and terms, you can move them around and get an equation that gives you total utility after taking action on a 100 gearboxes. The notion of the indifference line is that you have a fixed rule and you act on that rule. So here you would throw away 5% of all gear boxes just because you know 5% will be bad. Alternatively, you can keep every gear box, not throw away anything. Now you know that you will have 5% of your machines that are going to cost you $300 because they are bad. You can set a threshold at various levels of keeping everything or throwing everything away. And that's what gives you this utility or net benefit. region on your ROC curve. During model development, you might come across a model that has a lower area under the curve but crosses over the indifference line in a certain zone. So if you set your decision threshold there, you would make a better decision than by having a fixed rule. So that's the intuition. There are people who are interested solely in the accuracy or precision of their AI models and they generally ignore the fact that their models will have no clinical utility. A decision curve analysis can help understand which model likely has the highest clinical utility, which is not always the model with the highest accuracy.

This guide outlines the **total product life cycle** of artificial intelligence in medicine, shifting the focus from theoretical accuracy to the practical hurdles of **clinical integration**. ## 49. Type of Action in response to AI solution.mp4

Furthermore, certain models may address **population-level outcomes**, requiring a broader set of regulatory and governmental stakeholders to be effective. Ultimately, evaluating this **outcome-action pairing** is essential for identifying the specific resources and personnel required to transform an algorithmic prediction into a feasible, high-impact clinical reality.

**Keywords:** AI action types, Operational versus medical, Action lead time, Outcome action pairing, Population level action

### Content

You must clarify the type of action to evaluate the feasibility to implement the AI solution at point of care. Let's go back to our example of the AI solution that predicts 30-day readmission. after hospital discharge. An operational action paired with this outcome could be to schedule an appointment with a primary care provider 14 days after discharge for patients with high risk of readmission. The operational resources needed to act on this could include a hospital scheduler to call the patient and arrange the appointment or IT support who could automatically generate an email to schedule the appointment. An operational action requires different resources than a medical action. Using the same example, If we change the action to be additional prophylaxis treatment during impatient stay, you can imagine that different resources would be necessary to carry out this medical action. In this example, lead time would be long-term as the system would have several days to take action. In another example, if you are predicting acute kidney injury, your action would require a medical action and your window of time or lead time is likely acute. Deciding on the appropriate action is vital to assess the utility of an AI solution. We can walk through a few examples to illustrate this point. This is a representation of this concept with action types on the y-axis and action lead times on the x-axis. What if my AI solution predicts ICU transfer? Where would this fall on the grid? This could be an acute operational action as the hospital care team would need to act on this prediction by possibly arranging the transfer, finding a bed in ICU, and possibly identifying the ICU care team. If I am predicting cardiac arrest, this would be an acute medical action. Here, the care team would need to react to the prediction and immediately try to mitigate the outcome by administering a drug or performing an intervention. The lead time for this action would be acute. Alternatively, if my AI solution predicts a hospital readmission, This could be either an operational or medical action as we've previously discussed. If the action is to schedule an appointment with a primary care provider, the action is operational and long-term. However, if the action is to provide prophylaxis treatment, the action is medical and the action is also long-term because the team would have the entire impatient state to act upon the output. Before we leave this topic, there is another type of action that is worth mentioning here. Population level action.
